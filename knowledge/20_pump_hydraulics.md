Hydraulics

Definitions
• The term hydraulics refers to the study of the
behaviour of liquids (water) both when in motion and
when at rest.
• Specifically, hydrostatics refers to the study of fluids at
rest and hydrokinetics is the study of fluids in motion.

• One Newton is the force needed to accelerate one
kilogram of mass at the rate of one metre per second
squared in the direction of the applied force.

Atmospheric Pressure
The atmosphere, which envelops the earth
although gaseous, possesses definite weight and
exerts a pressure of 101,300 Newton’s per metre
squared uniformly over the surface of the earth. It
is important to understand the significance of
atmospheric pressure because the height to which
a pump can lift water, under suction from an open
source; the performance of the pump and the
operation of a siphon are all effected by it. The
Standard International unit of pressure is the
newton per square metre (N/m²) another name is
the Pascal. However, it has been decided that,
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> because this is a very small unit, the fire service
unit of pressure will be the ‘bar’. The relationship between these units is:
Normal atmospheric pressure = 101,300 Newton’s = 1.013 Bar. For the easy of calculations, it
has been rounded down to 1 Bar.

Suction Lift
Basic Facts
A pump is said to lift water when the water is taken from an open source below the inlet of the
pump. The suction lift is the vertical distance between the water surface and the centre line of
the impeller. Water has no tensile strength and cannot therefore be pulled upward and, as we
have already seen, it is only the atmospheric pressure which raises the water (see Fig. 9).

The purpose of a priming device, when water is being lifted on the suction side of a pump, is to
create a partial vacuum within the pump chamber and suction hose. The atmosphere exerts
pressure on the open surface of the water and so forces the water up through the suction hose
and into the pump. The mechanical condition of the pump directly affects its ability to create a
partial vacuum and in turn has a bearing on the total height of the suction lift.

Practical Limitations
When pumping from an open source it is the atmospheric pressure alone which can provide the
lift necessary for the water to enter the pump.

It has been shown that water cannot rise to a vertical height greater than approximately 10m in
a completely evacuated tube, and that it does so because it is forced up by the atmospheric
pressure acting on the water surface outside.

However, the atmospheric pressure’s ability to lift water is reduced to below the theoretical
capability of about 10m because it also must:

C.  Create flow i.e. to give the water kinetic energy as it changes from its static state in the
open supply to its moving state in the suction hose.

R.   Overcome frictional Resistance to flow in the suction hose and couplings.

E.  Overcome pressure loss due to turbulence and shock as the water enters the pump impeller.
It is known as Entry loss and varies with the design of the pump.

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> S.  Overcome pressure loss as the water is forced through the Strainer and changes its direction
of flow after entering.

T. Overcome any tendency for the water to vaporise (and so create an opposing vapour
pressure) as it nears the impeller. This tendency increases rapidly with Temperature. In fact, the
vapour pressure of water becomes equal to atmospheric pressure at its boiling point so making
it totally impossible to lift water at, or near, this temperature. At normal outdoor water
temperatures, the effect is small, however.
These five factors may conveniently be remembered by the mnemonic C.R.E.S.T

---

Factors (1), (2), (3) and (4) are each increased by increasing flowrate, and because the proportion
of atmospheric pressure available to deal with them decreases as lift increases, it follows that
the output from the pump will also decrease with increasing lift. Whilst suction lifts of 8.5m or
more are sometimes obtained under very good conditions, 8m can be considered the
approximate practical maximum if a worthwhile output is to be obtained from the pump.

To appreciate the impact of lift on pump performance consider a pump working from open water
with a suction lift of 3m as shown in Fig. 10 (Note that when manufacturers give information on
pump performance, it is common to specify a lift of 3m.) To do the work of raising the water to the
pump a pressure equivalent to 3m head of water is required. This, subtracted from a total
available pressure equivalent to 10m, leaves a pressure equivalent to 7m head to overcome the
various losses described above.

If, however, the suction lift was 8m (Fig. 11), a pressure equivalent to 8m head of water would
be required to raise the water to the pump, leaving only 2m head to overcome the losses.
Consequently, for any given diameter of suction, the quantity of water capable of being
delivered would be considerably less than that possible at a 3m suction lift.
(Note the lift distance is the distance from the water source to the pump, not the length of the
hose used to achieve the lift.)

Other factors that affect the efficiency and height of a suction lift include altitude and the quality
and condition of the pump

Friction Loss
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> As we have already stated, pressure in a fluid at rest is the same in all directions (a charged fire
hose for example). When water flows through a hose or a pipe there is a gradual loss of pressure
resulting from the need to overcome the frictional resistance which exists between the moving
water stream and the internal surface of the hose or pipe.
There are several laws governing friction loss and while accurate estimation of friction loss is
complicated it is more important to understand the physical factors that are most significant in
determining friction loss.

4 Laws governing loss of pressure due to friction

1. Friction loss is directly proportional to the length of the hose through which it is travelling

2. Friction loss is directly proportional to the “friction factor” for the hose (usually
determined by the roughness of the hose interior).

3. Friction loss is directly proportional to the square of the flow rate.
Therefore, if the flow rate through a hose is doubled the friction loss is increased
to the power of 4

4. Friction loss is inversely proportional to the power of five to the hose diameter.
If the diameter of the hose is halved the friction loss is approximately 32 times
greater (i.e. 25)

These points are of significant interest when

• Water is drawn from hydrants on small diameter mains
• When pumping water over long distances
• Where small diameter hose is being used

Jet Reaction
A basic law of physics states that for every action
there is an equal and opposite reaction.
When water is projected from a nozzle a reaction
equal and opposite to the force of the jet takes
place and tends to recoil in the opposite direction.
While it is possible for one person to hold a small
jet it will take two or more people to control a
large jet, as the reaction takes place on a large
mass of water.
If the diameter of the nozzle is double the quantity
of water passing through it is quadrupled.

Venturi

Water flowing through a venturi, i.e. a section of pipe in which the diameter gradually reduces
from its initial value, at point A, to a minimum, at the throat, B, before increasing again. Because
the changes in diameter are gradual, little turbulence is created in the water stream. As the
water flows from A to B its velocity, and hence its kinetic energy, increases to a maximum at the
expense of pressure which falls to a minimum value at B. At point C the kinetic energy has
decreased to its initial value (if the diameters at A and C are the same) and the pressure recovers
to close to its former value. If the pressure at C is not too high, it is possible for the pressure at
the throat of the venture to fall to below that of the atmosphere with the result that air, water
or any other fluid outside the device will be drawn (induced) into the stream through any
opening which may exist at the throat. This is the principle underlying the operation of some
types of flowmeters, the foam inductor and of the ejector pump.

Water hammer
This is a phenomenon with which most people will be familiar because it frequently occurs in
domestic situations such as when the flow of water through a long run of metal pipe is stopped
very quickly by the rapid closing of a tap. The consequent metallic 'clunk' which may be heard is
the consequence of 'water hammer'.

The force required to bring an object to rest is dependant inversely on the braking time – the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> shorter the time the greater the force. There are several fire ground situations where the time
taken to terminate a substantial mass of water moving with considerable velocity may be short
and consequently damage to equipment may result.

---

Examples of this are:

• Shutting down a branch rapidly
• The rapid closure of the hydrant to tank valve or the hydrant valve itself.

These examples indicate the necessity for slowly closing hydrants, valves
and branches to avoid water hammer which might burst hose;
damage couplings; pumps; collecting heads; tanks and water mains.
