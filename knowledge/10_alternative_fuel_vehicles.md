Section 4 Alternative Fuel Vehicles
4.1 Introduction.
Alternative fuel vehicles (AFVs) are vehicles that use fuels other than conventional petrol or
diesel. These vehicles use alternative energy sources like electricity, natural gas, hydrogen, or
biofuels to power their engines. AFVs are gaining popularity due to increasing fuel costs and
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> environmental concerns. As with all RTCs a DRA must be performed, and warning signs would
be sparks, smoke, electric or sulphur smells. Except for the precautions listed in this section,
AFVs may be approached using standard vehicle extrication principles and techniques.
Additional information can be obtained from rescuers guides published by the various vehicle
manufacturers or in various rescue apps.

Electric vehicle accidents present unique challenges for rescue personnel due to factors
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> like high-voltage battery risks, potential for thermal runaway and fires, and the heavier
weight of EVs compared to traditional vehicles. These factors can increase the risk of
electrocution, fire hazards, and more severe injuries in collisions.

4.4 Liquefied and compressed natural gas fuelled vehicles
LPG, LNG and CNG are very similar to each other. LNG is frozen into a liquid form and takes
up less space than CNG, which is pressurised to the point where it is very compact and is
safer than LNG as it will dissipate in the event of a leak. Both are most common in HGVs.
LPG, or Liquefied Petroleum Gas, consists of propane and butane and can be liquefied under
moderate pressure at ambient temperatures.
4.4.1 Compressed Natural Gas. (CNG)
Most common in HGV Tractor units and can carry between 750 – 1000 litres charged at 200
bar. Dublin port is the only refuelling facility in Ireland.

4.4.2 Liquefied Natural Gas. (LNG)
Liquefied Natural Gas is stored at a temperature of approximately -160C. The LNG must be
stored and transported permanently at around this temperature, and this is accomplished by
super insulation in a pressurized double tank system. It is similar in principle to a thermos
flask, together with a venting system to remove any excess pressure which may be produced.
The storage pressure of about 8 bar is not regarded as very high but because of the insulation
requirements tanks are large. Therefore, the use of this fuel is only suited to large, heavy
diesel vehicles such as trucks, buses and HGV.

---

4.4.3 Liquefied Petroleum Gas. (LPG)
Vehicles fuelled by LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas) are relatively rare in Ireland. Most
commonly, the storage tanks (which may contain up to 200 litres of fuel) are located in the
rear section or the boot compartment of vehicles. However, they may be located in the engine
compartment, alongside the wings, or in the load space, immediately behind the front seats
in commercial vehicles. There may also be more than one tank.
LPG systems may be fitted in the following ways:
• Factory fitted systems
• Vehicles converted post-delivery by approved specialists
• Vehicles converted by non-approved installers
• Systems installed by individuals using DIY kits.

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> It is not always immediately evident that a vehicle is fuelled by LPG. It is also incorrect to
assume that there will be a secondary fill cap located elsewhere on the vehicle. Some Volvo
models install the LPG filler behind the alternative fuel filler. Therefore, only one fuel filler cap
is provided.

Note: Until determined otherwise, all vehicles involved in fire should be treated as LPG-fuelled
vehicles.

4.4.4 Hazards associated with LPG-fuelled vehicles
A professionally installed system should be fitted with a pressure relief valve (PRV). In the
event of a fire and the valve operating correctly, there will be a powerful jet of flame 5–7
metres in length which will pulse from the relief valve for up to 20 minutes, depending on the
amount of LPG in the tank. Any pulsing or constant flame from a pressure relief device is likely
to project from the front or rear of the vehicle, dependant on the location of the storage tank.
This flame will pose an obvious risk to fire fighters who should only approach from the vehicle
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> sides if necessary.  Regardless of the system involved, the main hazard presented to fire
fighters, at fires involving vehicles fitted with LPG fuel systems, is the possibility of the tank
undergoing a boiling liquid expanding vapour explosion (BLEVE).  A BLEVE can occur when an
LPG tank is subjected to heat from an external source. It is more likely to happen if the system
has been fitted by an individual using a DIY kit, or by a nonapproved installer, as there may
not be a pressure relief valve fitted to the system. However, even with a pressure relief valve
fitted to the system and operating correctly, it may not prevent the tank from rupturing.  The
LPG tank can withstand a build-up of pressure so long as the metal retains its strength. Even
with continued relief valve operation, the liquid level drops, exposing greater areas of the
metal to heat. Flame impinging on the dry wall of the tank will create temperatures high
enough to weaken the metal, leading potentially to failure of the tank. Tank failure is
immediately followed by the release of boiling LPG liquid, the effect of which is the flash
evaporation of the liquid. Ignition of the expanding vapour cloud creates a blast, flash and
fireball. The cylinder fragments may be propelled over 200 metres.
Note: It is possible for a tank correctly fitted with a pressure relief valve (PRV) to still rupture.
If there is direct localised flame impingement on a cylinder, then the vapour will be
expanding at a far greater rate than the PRV can vent.

4.4 Hydrogen Cell fuelled vehicles
The National Transport Authority (NTA) are responsible for the purchase of all public
transport vehicles in Ireland. AS of the 15/08/2025 there are currently 3 x double decker
public service hydrogen cell buses on trial in Ireland operated by Bus Éireann. They are on
the 105X route, which runs between Dublin and Ratoath, County Meath. Hydrogen for the
buses will initially be supplied by BOC Gases Ireland, with refuelling at its Bluebell facility on
the Naas Road in Dublin. Dublin Bus is exploring the possibility of using hydrogen cell buses
but at present the storage of hydrogen fuel is the main issue they are facing.

There are other commercial buses and HGVs already on the road.

4.4.1 Considerations at hydrogen cell vehicle incidents
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Hazard identification, identify vehicle fuel types and use Emergency response
guidebook if required.
• Mark sure the vehicle is put into park mode and chocked.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Isolation and stabilisation, isolate vehicle using emergency shut offs.
• Be aware of HV and High-pressure lines and pressure relief devices.
• Vehicle access – avoid cutting HV cables or hydrogen lines. (stainless steel)
• Be aware of electrocution risks.
