Compressed Air Foam System (C.A.F.S.)
Introduction
Firefighting foam is a stable mass of small bubbles of lower density that most
flammable liquids and water. Foam is a blanketing and cooling agent that is produced
by mixing air into a foam solution that contains water and foam concentrate.

Foam extinguishes flammable or combustible liquid fires in 4 ways:

1. Excludes air from the flammable vapours (smothers)
2. Eliminates vapour release from the fuel surface (fuel barrier)
3. Separates flames from the fuel surface (heat barrier)
4. Cools the fuel surface and surrounding surfaces (cooling)

Fig. 1 Illustrates how foam extinguishes fires involving fuels

The diagrams below illustrate how foam is made through a typical configuration
using a delivery hose, a pickup tube and proportioning device. The first shows
foam, which has mixed with air using a venturi style foam branch, which is known
as, aspirated foam. The second is not mixed with air and is known as non-
aspirated foam.

Compressed Air Foam Systems:
Compressed Air Foam Systems (C.A.F.S.) are designed to produce aspirated
finished foam without the need of a foam-making branch. CAFS consists of a water
pump, a foam concentrate injection pump and an air compressor, which combine

---

to produce aerated foam at the delivery of the pump. CAFS can be appliance or
trailer mounted and can be supplied with a range of water pumps, concentrate
injection pumps and different air compressors depending on the requirements of
the user
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Tests have shown that CAFS can throw foam further than conventional UK fire service
foam- making branches whilst producing a well-worked low expansion foam.

The system also produced a medium expansion foam with foam that was very sticky
and could be used to coat vertical surfaces. When using Class A foam concentrates, it
is claimed that the ability of the foam stick to vertical surfaces will cool and insulate
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> the material and prevent fire spread from radiated heat. CAFS may also be used with
other types of foam concentrates such as Class B, which is used in Tunnel Firefighting
in the Dublin Port Tunnel. Expansion ratios are adjustable between 7:1 and 30:1

Disadvantages of Using CAFS
•
Costly
•
Successful foam production requires the pump operator to follow a
specific prescribed sequence which can delay the delivery of the
medium
•
At temperatures of around 394°F (200°C), hoses fail sooner when filled with
CAFS than when filled with water. Reduced water contents reduce the heat
capacity of the extinguishing media

Types of C.A.F.S.
Class A CAFS Foam
This foam is used on solid combustible materials with an application rate of
between 0.1% and 1.0%.

Properties of Class A CAFS
•
The wetting effectiveness of the water is increased giving it the ability to
penetrate and soak into Class “A” fuels. The National Institute of Standards
and Technology has estimated that water having been treated with Class “A”
foam concentrate can wet a Class “A” fuel up to 20 times more rapidly than
untreated water.
•
The water also has a foaming ability. The foam bubble will adhere to
vertical or three-dimensional surfaces longer than plain water. This allows
the “wetter” water in the bubble a chance to soak into the Class “A” fuel.
•
Foam bubbles create greater surface area for more rapid heat
absorption than a droplet while also decreasing run off. The benefit to
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> the fire fighter is faster extinguishment, less water usage and less heat
stress.
•
The National Institute of Standards and Technology states that water
treated with a Class “A” foam concentrate makes the water 3 to 5 times
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> more efficient at fire extinguishment than untreated water.

Class A CAFS should not be used on Class B fires as although it may result in some
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> initial success in extinguishing the fire, the foam blanket will potentially decompose
or mix with the fuel which will not suppress the vapours and could result in a re-
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> ignition or an explosion.

Class B CAFS Foam
This foam can be used on flammable liquids with an application
rate of between 1% and 6% and is also known as alcohol-
resistant aqueous film forming foam (AR-AFFF). The DFB uses an
application rate of 3% in DN14A5 and DN14A1. The composition
of foam is often up to 90% water and therefore the alcohol
resistant element is essential when treating fires involving polar
solvents. Hydrocarbons such as crude oil, paraffin’s, gasoline or
kerosene are not water miscible. However polar solvents such as
alcohols and ethers will mix readily with water and can be highly
destructive to foam blankets.

Bounce Off Technique:
For straight stream use, the foam should be banked off a wall or other obstruction
where available

Fig. 6 Bounce Off Technique

Rain down Technique:
The foam branch is directed almost straight up, and the foam stream is allowed to
reach its maximum height and break into small droplets. The branch operator must
adjust the altitude of the branch so that the fallout pattern matches that of the spill
area. This technique is less effective where weather conditions are unfavorable or
where there has been significant pre-burn and a thermal column has developed.

Standard Tests
Pumps (vehicle mounted and portable)

Although Firefighters are not normally responsible for the routine maintenance of pumps, in
the interest of a long trouble-free pump life, attention should be paid to the following:

•
Strainers must be used where appropriate and checked and cleaned afterwards.
This is to eliminate stones or other objects entering and damaging the pump
•
Following pumping from salt or polluted water the pump, primer, hose reels, water
tank and pipework should be thoroughly flushed with clean water to prevent
corrosion. The same should apply when using Compressed Air Foam.
•
The manufacturer recommends that in cold weather the pump should be drained
of all water to prevent possible freezing. In practical terms this is not feasible, but
every effort should be made to house the pump where exposure to the elements
is limited. Where fitted, pump bay heaters should be used.
•
On portable pumps, for any reason the mixture of water and anti-freeze fluid for
the cooling system is spilt onto the pump or its frame, it should be carefully washed
off and dried.

The only requirement for the testing of pumps is that which is laid down in Technical Bulletin
1/1994. It is a quarterly test for both vehicle mounted and portable pumps as is described in
this section.

Note: Total head is the delivery pressure minus the inlet pressure (which should be a negative

Quarterly Dry Vacuum Test

“It is anticipated that this test will normally be conducted by station personnel.
An initial visual inspection of all the lengths of suction hose to undergo testing shall be
conducted.
All the lengths of suction hose that have been visually inspected shall then be coupled to the
suction inlet of the pump with a blank cap in place on the end of the final length and with the
blank caps removed from all deliveries. The pump shall then be operated at the specified
priming speed in order to obtain - 0.7 bar vacuum which shall be achieved within 45 seconds.
This vacuum should be maintained for at least 15 seconds.”
Failure to achieve the above performance indicates an excessive air leak within the system
which may be due to leakage at pressure gauge connections, delivery valves, couplings hose
etc.”
The matter should be reported to the Officer in Charge and referred to the Maintenance
Department for further tests/repair.
Any other testing is carried out in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions.

Pump Vacuum Test (Prima)
Place the blanking cap(s) in position on the inlet(s) of the pump and close the delivery
valves. Run the pump at 1300-1500 rpm and observe the vacuum/compound needle.
When a vacuum of - 0.81 bar is obtained, stop the pump. This vacuum should be
maintained for at least 15 seconds or drop no more than 0.07bar in a minute.
If the pump will not hold the vacuum with the blanking caps in position, a leak is present
in the pump, and the ‘pump pressure test’ detailed below must be carried out to trace it.
Should the pump not reach a vacuum of - 0.81 bar but will hold a lower pressure, a fault in
the priming system is indicated.

Pump Vacuum Test (World Series)
Place the blanking cap(s) in position on the inlet(s) of the pump and close the delivery valves.
Run the pump at 1000 - 2000 rpm and observe the compound needle. When a vacuum of -
0.7 bar is obtained, stop the pump. This vacuum should be maintained for at least 15 sec.
If a Water Ring Primer is fitted follow the same procedure but run the pump at 2,800 rpm.
If the pump will not hold the vacuum with the blanking caps in position, a leak is
present in the pump, and the ‘pump pressure test’ detailed below must be carried out to trace
it.
Should the pump not reach a vacuum of - 0.7 bar but will hold a lower pressure, a fault
in the priming system is indicated.
Check as follows:
Check each primer drain hole for water leakage. If leakage is found, report to the Officer in
Charge and refer to the Maintenance Department for further tests/repair.
If the pump will not achieve - 0.7 bar vacuum, and will not hold what it does achieve,
there is a leak, and possibly also a fault, in the priming system.

Pump Pressure Test (without pump running)
This test needs only to be carried out if the pump will not hold the vacuum with blanking
cap(s) in position and is intended to trace the leaks responsible for the loss of vacuum.
Apply a water pressure of 3.5 – 7.0 bar to the pump and check for leaks. The area causing the
leaks should it be visible. Should for example the leak be as simple as securely closing a
delivery valve or draincock, the issue may be resolved. Some other leaks from within the
pump cannot be rectified so easily such as a leaking primer. If no leaks are apparent the
leakage may lie between the priming valve and the primer. These issues should be reported
to the Officer in Charge and referred to the Maintenance Department for further tests/repair.

Portable Pumps
Introduction
All Brigades have, as part of their pumping capacity, pumps which can be manhandled into
position which are known as Light Portable Pumps (LPPs). These are usually carried on
appliances and are especially useful in areas where vehicles cannot get to water supplies. All
have carrying frames with handles and can be carried by 2 or 4 persons. Most are driven by
an internal combustion engine.
The current Irish and European Standard for portable pumps is IS/EN 14466:2005 + A1:2008.

Different Water Supplies
In general, the different water supplies available for firefighting are as follows
• Appliance Water Tank (typically 1800lts)
• Water Mains Systems
• Hydrants
• Dedicated Fixed Firefighting Water Systems
• Open Water Sources
• Mobile Water Supplies

Corrosion and Tuberculation
Please note that Corrosion and Tuberculation
can develop on metal water supply pipes. This can lead
to structural integrity issues and flow disruption by
increasing friction loss and reducing the flow rate
