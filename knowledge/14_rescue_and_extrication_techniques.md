Section 8 Rescue and Extrication Techniques
8.1 INTRODUCTION
Upon arrival at an incident, personnel will be confronted with a number of factors that will
have to be taken into account before attempting to extricate casualties from vehicles.  At all
R.T.C.’s the nature of the casualty’s condition should determine the nature of the rescue
method employed.
8.2 Extraction Methods
Prior to commencing physical rescue activities, it is essential that.
• Vehicles have been adequately stabilised
• The glass has been safely “managed”
• Hard protection is available for placement between tool and casualty.

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> 8.2.1 Vehicle stabilisation
Vehicles are designed to provide passengers with a comfortable “ride” regardless of road
conditions. Suspension and shock absorber units are central in preventing road vibration
being transmitted through the wheels to the vehicles occupants. It is this potential movement
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> of the vehicles on its suspension system during rescue efforts that stabilisation is aimed at
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> avoiding.  In many instances, bystanders and other emergency services attending may already
have entered vehicles prior to the arrival of the Fire Service. These vehicles must be stabilised.

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> 8.2.1.1 Objectives of stabilisation
1. Immobilise a vehicle resting in a dangerous position.
2. Result in a solid platform being created for rescuers.
3. Be simple and easy to remember.
4. Require very little setting up time. As an example, any typical car resting on all four
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> wheels should take no longer than forty-five seconds to stabilise.

8.2.1.2 Step Chocks

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> One excellent device used to stabilise vehicles is the step chock or “staged wedge”.  This is a
wooden or plastic assembly constructed in a step configuration. Used around the world by
rescue crews, they make excellent use of valuable stowage space and assist in the rapid
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> stabilisation of vehicles.

A number of different methods of stabilising vehicles are described below. This should allow
for the wide variety of blocks, chocks and wedges carried in each individual fire brigade.
Method 1 - Flat Tyre Blocking
One method of stabilising a vehicle on all four wheels is so called “Flat Tyre Blocking” which
is used in a number of countries worldwide. With this method, step chocks are placed under
the vehicle to rest on an appropriate “step” thereby taking away all movement from the
vehicle’s suspension.

Flat Tyre Blocking
NOTE:
While it may be an effective method of stabilising vehicles, “Flat Tyre Blocking” has 2 main
disadvantages. First, it should be noted that tyre pressures form an important part in police
accident investigations into R.T.C’s and by altering them, this important evidence is lost.
Where flat tyre blocking is used, it is essential that the police are informed. In addition, it is
often necessary to move a vehicle at some point after the initial collision. If the tyres have

been deflated this becomes difficult to achieve. For these reasons, the following alternative
methods are considered more appropriate.
Method 2
Place 2 step chocks each side of the car, one behind the front wheel, the other in front of the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> rear wheel to provide stabilisation at all four points. Tap home a wedge to secure each step
chock fast against the vehicle’s sills.

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Stabilisation method using step chocks and wedges
With today’s new car construction characteristics, this placement of step chocks will help to
prevent undue movement of the vehicle should the roof be removed, thereby reducing the
structural integrity of the vehicle.
Where there is a risk of further movement due to the vehicle resting on a slope and a hand
brake cannot be applied, blocks and wedges should be placed on appropriate wheels.
Method 3
Should step chocks not be available to the rescuer, blocks as available are placed close to the
front and rear wheels on either side of the vehicle as close as possible to the underside of the
sills. A wedge is tapped home to hold the blocks fast against the sill, thereby reducing any risk
of the vehicle moving on its suspension.

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> “Stabilisation method using blocks and wedges”.

8.3 Primary Roof Removal
The advantages of primary roof removal are.
• It creates the maximum amount of space to work in, in the minimum amount of time,
invariably facilitating full access for the medical assessment of casualties.
• It gives unrestricted access to a casualty’s airway and allows for in-car intubation
should the need arise (impossible with the roof on).
• It facilitates the placement of spinal immobilisation devices

All cuts with the exception of the “C” posts should be made as low as possible to reduce the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> hazard from sharp metal. These should then be covered. “C” posts should be cut at their
narrowest point.  The last cut should be made nearest to the casualty in order that there is
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> no danger of an unsupported roof dropping and causing injury. It also frees other personnel
from having to support it from first cut to last.
Note: if a side fold down is then to follow, the “B” pillar should be cut high to provide extra
leverage.
Where possible, roofs should be removed totally as opposed to merely “flapping” them back.
Roof flaps are, however, useful in allowing rapid access to the interior of the vehicle -
particularly when a full removal entails cutting two wide “C” posts.  They should, none-the-
less, be viewed as the first stage of a full roof removal. The correct method of flapping roofs
is to cut both “A” posts (low down). Protect the casualty from glass fragments then join these
cuts using a glass-master saw. Cut both “B” posts then make two further relief cuts either side
of the roof, adjacent the “C” post and “flap” the roof back, it is often useful to lie a ceiling
hook across the roof. Two rescuers can then bear their weight down either end of the hook
whilst further rescuers flap the roof over it.

---

Roof Flap

Most modern vehicles have bonded, laminated front windscreens with several different
methods being used to bond the glass to the roof structure. This type of windscreen should
not add to the time taken in removing a roof. Assuming a driver entrapment, this could be
achieved by the following evolution:

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Ensure the casualty is protected from glass fragments.
• Expose for new car technology.
• Commence cutting at the driver’s side “A” pillar (low).
• Continue in an anticlockwise direction, finishing with the last cut adjacent the casualty
(driver’s side “B” post).
• As soon as the two “A” posts are cut, the cuts are joined by cutting through the
laminated glass in a straight line along the bottom of the windscreen. This is done
while the remaining roof pillars are cut and should not, therefore, add to the time
taken to remove the roof.

8.4 Side Entry
As a general rule, door removals should begin at the front hinge, casualty side. The first
problem to overcome is in gaining access to the door hinges as there is often insufficient space
to allow the tips of the spreaders to be inserted.

Another method is to place the tool in the window opening with the jaws facing downwards,
either directly adjacent the hinges or adjacent the lock. When the tool is closed the door skins
will crush together producing a small gap between the door and either the front or rear wing.
That gap can then be exploited to burst either the lock or the hinges.

---

By squeezing the two door skins together access can be gained either to the lock or the hinges.
When attempting to burst the hinges the tool should be inserted above the top hinge,
encouraging the door to fold down and away from the casualty inside.

To carry out the complete removal of the sides of 4 door vehicles, two methods are
recommended.  Either a side “fold down” or a “B” post rip.
8.5 Side Fold Down
When opting to remove the side using the side fold down method, the “B” post of the side to
be removed should be cut high - close to the roof. (This provides extra leverage during the
actual “fold down”). Both the front hinges adjacent to the front wing and the rear door lock
are forced using the techniques previously described.  Cuts are then made either side of the
bottom of the “B” post. Often this is sufficient itself to remove the whole of the side complete,
if not, pull the side down by levering on the “B” post and complete the side removal by further
selective cutting at the base of the “B” post.
8.5.1 The “B Post Rip”
An alternative method of removing the side of a 4-door vehicle is the “B” post rip.  This
method has the advantage of not requiring any hinges to be burst and can therefore be less
time consuming. It is also the most suitable method of side removal where there is side
damage resulting in bodywork being forced in towards the casualty. With side impact
damage, attempts to force either the lock or the hinges will result in metal work being forced
further in towards the casualty - with obvious consequences. The “B” post rip has the
advantage of only forcing wreckage away from the casualty and therefore is the obvious
choice.

---

The following evolution is shown below. Begin by attacking the rear lock if this is not already
openable. Cut the seat belt. Cut the top of the “B” post. (this should already have been done
to remove the roof). Next a cut is made at the bottom of the “B” post parallel to the sill. It is
not necessary to cut the “B” post all the way through.  Finally, the tips of the spreaders are
placed between the sill and the base of the rear door. By opening up the spreaders the initial
cut in the base of the “B” post is further “ripped” until the “B” post is finally torn away from
the sill. Both doors and “B” post can then be swung round to the front of the vehicle and
either secured by line or seat belt or removed altogether.

8.6 Forced Pedal Pull
Casualties can be found with their feet trapped by pedals. Although specialist hydraulic pedal
cutters exist in brigades, because they cut with a shearing action which can cause the loose
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> end of the pedal to fly off, they may not always be the preferred rescue method. Two further
methods may prove of value to rescuers.

Pedal Pull
The picture above shows a simple method of achieving a forced pedal pull. A length of seat
belt is attached to an appropriate pedal by a clove hitch. The door is then closed, almost, and
several turns of the running end of the seat belt are made around the door pillar. On opening
the door, the pedal is forced upwards and away from the casualty’s foot. Either door can be
used depending on which pedal is required to be forced.
8.7 DASH ROLL
From time-to-time rescuers are faced with a casualty trapped by the steering wheel,
dashboard and front fascia. The dash roll is the recommended extrication method to employ.
In the description and illustrations that follow, it will be noted that the roof and doors of the
vehicle have already been removed - as will be the case if space has been created
systematically.
Two relief cuts are made at the bottom of each of the “A” posts parallel to the sills. Under no
circumstances should these cuts be made in the sill itself as this can result in the floor pan
rising up and further trapping the casualty’s feet.

---

Relief Cut at the bottom of the “A” Post

The spreaders is closed onto the sill close to the “B” post, casualties’ side, for use as a “stop”.
A Ram is then placed between (1) the angle of the “A” post at the join with the roof pillar and
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> (2) the spreader “stops”. Additional stabilisation must then be provided underneath the ram
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> to ensure the floor pan doesn’t collapse when the ram is extended.

Dash Roll & Sill Support

Under the supervision of the O.I.C. the ram is then extended causing the relief cuts to tear
further and the dash, steering wheel and fascia to “roll” away from the casualty. As soon as
this happens, it is essential that the packing close to the front wheels is adjusted and that a
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> wedge is inserted progressively into the relief cut to ensure that in the event of either tool
slippage or failure, there is no possibility of the dash dripping back onto the casualty.
If this packing is adjusted correctly, when full extension is reached the ram can be removed.
Full and unrestricted access to the casualty can then be had from the side of the vehicle.

8.8 Entry via the Boot
Although at first sight this might not appear to be a particularly obvious space creation
technique, it can be extremely useful when more conventional roof removal is impracticable.
A series of cuts using either dedicated cutters or metal cutting saws will allow the number plate sill to
be folded down. Removal of rear seat squab and parcel shelf requires surprisingly little selective
cutting and is usually easily achieved.

8.9 Roof Fold Down
Rescuers faced with a vehicle coming to rest on its side following a collision invariably view
this as a “difficult” rescue. Surprisingly, it is one of the easier to achieve if approached
systematically. As with other extrications, the priorities are very much on stability, rapid initial
access to casualties, glass management and then space creations. (Maximum space in
minimal time). The obvious answer in this instance being to cut and fold the roof down.

The sequence being as follows:
• Cuts are made to the A, B, and C posts. These cuts should be made high rather than
low as in a conventional roof removal.
• As the casualty’s position frequently precludes the cutting of the other A, B, and C
posts, two relief cuts are then made in the roof close to the A and C posts.
• The windscreen is cut between the A post cut and the relief cut.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • The roof is then folded down and by having cut the pillars high, there is no trip hazard
present.
• Once the roof has been folded down, rescuers will have full access to the casualties
inside. The final extrication is usually quite straightforward.

8.10 Cars on their roofs
As with cars on their sides, vehicles coming to rest on their roofs frequently appear more
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> difficult than they are. The principles of rapid stabilisation and casualty access should again
be followed by adequate space creation. The illustration below depicts, either of the two side
removal techniques described earlier will prove satisfactory. This should be complemented
by rescuers inside the vehicle reclining the seats in order to provide maximum space in which
to work.

Car on its roof – B Post Rip

In a small minority of cases the roof of the vehicle may also have “Pancaked”, further
restricting space inside the passenger compartment. In this scenario, space creation becomes
something of a challenge. Selective cutting of A, B and C posts on the one side of the vehicle
together with appropriate relief cuts will enable rams to be inserted to force the vehicle up
and away from its roof. By creating space in this way, the final treatment and removal of
casualties is usually straightforward.
