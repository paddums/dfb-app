Collector pumping.
A third method of supplementing water supplies which is of value on occasions is known as
'collector pumping'. This may be briefly described as the assembling of several weak water
supplies into a single pump to produce one really effective supply.
When several hydrants on a main of low pressure are opened simultaneously, the flow from
each is reduced progressively with the number in use in such circumstances it is more
satisfactory to connect several hydrants by hose lines to a single, large capacity, pump fitted
with a suction collection head. A pump used in this way is known as a collector pump and is
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> positioned as close to the fire as fire-fighting considerations permit. This also provides for
much greater and more expeditious operational control of the delivery hose lines.

Crackling Jets
These are caused by air being drawn into the pump together with water. The air is then
compressed to the same pressure as the water. When it leaves the branch, it expands again
with slight explosive force causing the characteristic noise. It is generally caused by the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> strainer not being adequately submerged or a slight leak on the suction side.
Cavitation
When pumping from an open supply, the pressure on the inlet side of the pump can be so
low that the water can vaporise at the ambient temperature. Vapour bubbles then form
(this is known as cavitation) and the pump gives off a distinctive rattling sound. This will
indicate to the operator that the maximum flow condition has been reached, and he
should ease back the throttle until the noise just disappears.
The vacuum gauge gives no indication that cavitation conditions are reached because it
registers the general suction condition in the suction inlet of the pump and is not affected by
the low-pressure pockets which form at the entry to the impeller and the back surfaces of the
impeller blades
Water Relay
A water relay comprises several pumps spaced at intervals along a route between a water
source and the point where the water is required. The pumps are connected by lines of hose
through which the water is passed from one pump to the next until the discharged point is
reached. The systems in general use are listed below. Water relaying is covered in greater
detail within its own section in this book.
• Closed Circuit
• Open Circuit
(Including the Porter Relay)
Capacity of Pumps
The aim, when organising a water relay, is to deliver the maximum possible quantity of water
according to the capacity of the available pumps and using the minimum equipment. To a
water relay, this pressure energy is used up in two ways.
• In overcoming the static head/height against which the water must be delivered.
• In overcoming the friction loss in the hose through which the water must pass.
It has been stated that the loss of head due to friction varies as the square of the velocity of
the water in the hose.

Water Relaying
Where water supplies for firefighting are inadequate or non-existent at incidents, provision
of water is difficult and time consuming. A water relay comprises of a number of pumps
spaced at intervals along a route between a water source and the point where the water is
required. The pumps are connected by lines of hose through which the water is passed from
one pump to the next until the discharge point is reached.
There are 2 systems in general use:
• Open Circuit
• Closed Circuit
(Including the Porter Relay)
Before moving on to the different types of water relays. It is important to emphasise good
planning is essential before you make down.
Some of the considerations are:
• Topography of the area
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Distance to fire ground.
• Volumes of water required
• Sufficient resources and personnel to achieve the make down
• Appropriately sized and positioned pumping units
• Good communications systems in place
• Road Traffic Management if required

This list is not exhaustive and needs to be assessed in the incident management plan by the
Incident Commander/Water Officer.

Open Circuit Relay
One method of pumping water over long distances is known as an open relay wherein the
base pump delivers the water to a fixed or mobile container such as a canvas dam. From here
the water is picked up by another pump and relayed to another container where the process
is repeated as necessary. Therefore, each pump is an independent unit pumping from open
water but relying on the preceding pump to maintain the water level in the container.

---

Advantages

The flow can be maintained for a period even in the event of the base pump or a relay pump
becoming inoperative because there is a water supply still available in the dams. This allows
time to replace the damaged or inoperative pump.

Disadvantages

This system requires considerable time, equipment and personnel to be effective and
consequently a closed-circuit system is often preferred.

The flowrate from a water relay depends on:
• The performance of the pumps used
• The ability of the hose to convey the water
The output of the relay will depend on the output of the smallest capacity pump in the system.

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**

longest stage
Practical Considerations
There are several factors that might affect the success of a closed-circuit water relay which
need to be taken into consideration.
• The most effective pump needs to be placed at the source of the water
• The flowrate will be determined by the weakest pump in the relay
• Each pump’s ability to deliver water should be matched with the capacity of the hose
to convey it. There is no point in using large capacity pumps to deliver water through
long lengths of small diameter hose

---

• The number of hose lines and the size of the hose lines available will greatly influence
the flowrate
• Undulating land or gradient will also affect the flowrate.

Advantages of the Closed-Circuit Relay

It is generally more suitable than a standard relay because of its economy in equipment used
and the speed of getting to work. Also, a continuous flow of water can be provided for the
base pump through to the discharge pump and this flow can be continued during all
breakdowns except in the case of a burst length of hose. To be fully effective, however, the
closed-circuit relay depends on a high standard of pump operating.

The Porter Relay
This is a closed-circuit relay that incorporates a system where the number of appliances
arriving initially is limited and more appliances arrive to gradually increase its capacity but
without the need to interrupt the water supply. This arrangement was originally conceived
for 2250 lpm pumps which each carried 14 lengths of 70mm hose but there is no reason why
the principle could not be adapted to work with other equipment.

Undulating Ground and Gradient
It frequently happens that the ground over which a relay is laid is not level and if the gradients
concerned are sufficiently great some adjustment of the distances between pumps becomes
necessary.
Where the ground between the pumps is uphill, some of the pump pressure will be used up
in raising the water to the higher level with the result that less pressure will be available to
overcome frictional resistance in the hose. It may be possible to increase pump pressure to
compensate for this loss, provided the pump is not already working at full throttle but
otherwise the intended flowrate can be maintained by reducing the distance between the
pumps. Conversely where the ground between the pumps is downhill, the pumps can be set
further apart. An appropriate method for determining the spacing is to estimate the
difference in height between adjacent pumps in metres and divide this by 10 to give the
static head requirement in bar. This figure should then be divided by the operating pressure
of the relay and the distance between pumps reduced or increased by the resulting fraction.
We know that for every 10 metres of height or head 0.1 bar of pressure is lost so by dividing
the difference in height between adjacent pumps by 10 the relative pressure difference in bar
can be calculated.
For example, if there is a 20-metre height difference between adjacent pumps pumping uphill
the pressure differential will 20/10 = 2 bar. Therefore if 7 bar is the optimum pump pressure
the relay will need to be adjusted as follows:
(A) If pumping uphill 200m with height difference of 20m reduce distance by 2/7

Positioning the Base Pump and First Relay Pump
The output from the base pump, which must supply the water, will control the flow
throughout the relay. Ideally the most efficient pump should be used as the base pump. In
order to maximise the output from this pump the suction lift should ideally be no more than
3 metres using no more than 3 lengths of suction hose. In addition, the base pump will be
using part of its energy lifting the water and therefore the distance between it and the first

Communications
For the efficient operation of a water relay, it is important to maintain good communications
along the route so that changes in conditions and orders can be acted upon quickly. At larger
incidents one officer may be delegated responsibility for monitoring and managing the water
supplies and where radio communications are used a dedicated channel should be considered
for the officer and operators.

and the base pump operator should increase the pressure to 7 bar. The compound gauge on

> Delivering water to the fire ground is not always straightforward. There are many different
scenarios where the water source may be a considerable distance away, over undulating
ground or where the water source itself may be insufficient. Being able to overcome these

RTPP Foam System
The Godiva Prima/World range of pumps can be fitted with a Round the Pump foam system
(RTPP) capable of inducing up to 120 litres per minute of foam compound into the pump.
The system is compact, self-contained and is mounted between the pump suction tube,
volute and discharge manifold.

Foam can be delivered from both Low- and High-Pressure outlets
Foam Compound
The RTPP system is suitable for all commercially available Protein, Fluoroprotein and
Aqueous Film-Forming Foam (AFFF) compounds, commonly called Class B foams. Operation
with thixotropic alcohol resistant foams will cause inaccuracy in metering.
DFB appliances with RTPP have various sizes of on-board foam concentrate tanks. Pump
operators should be familiar with their appliances foam concentrate storage capacity.

Operation
The RTPP foam system is suitable for use when the pump is being supplied with water from
1. Vehicle mounted tank
2. Free standing tank or open water
3. Pressure fed source (Driving Ratio will need to be accounted for refer to the
manufactures manual for calculations)
•
High Pressures may exceed optimum branch Pressure.
•
High Pressure mains can damage water and foam Tanks
•
DFB currently generates RTPP foam from tank feed or open-source water
supplies only.
The system is very simple to operate. First check the amount of water flow discharging
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> through the branch nozzles in use. With the Prima pump water is always circulating through
the RTPP system but foam solution is not mixed until the foam supply valve is opened and
foam concentrate induced into the water flow.

The high-pressure foam system incorporates a 3-way valve in the foam supply line
(see Fig.7). The positions on this valve are –

1. Off – no foam or flushing water supplied.
2. Foam on – foam supplied to metering unit
3. Flushing water on – water is supplied from the low-pressure section of
the pump to flush the high-pressure foam system after use.

The 3-way valve is located on the side of the high/low pressure filter housing at the top of
the pump (left hand side for clockwise rotation pumps, right hand side for anticlockwise
rotation pumps). Turn the valve to Foam on position. Foam is supplied via an independent
HPFA foam metering unit @ 3%

Flushing
Flush – water is supplied from the low-pressure side of the pump to flush the high-pressure
foam system after use.

When LP Foam delivery is complete –
•
Close off Foam Tank and/or Pickup tube.
•
Open Foam Flow regulator to 120LPM
•
Flush system for a minimum of 15 minutes,
or until water runs clear from both HPFA & LP deliveries.
•
Return foam Flow regulator to Zero
•
When HPFA hose reel use is complete –
•
Select Flush on 3-way Valve
•
Flush system for a minimum of 15 minutes, or until water runs clear from both
HPFA & LP deliveries.
•
Return 3-way valve to OFF
