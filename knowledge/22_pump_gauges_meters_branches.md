Pressure Gauge Construction

The main types of Pressure Gauge construction are:

1. Bourdon tube

2. Diaphragm

Bourdon Tube
• The pressure responsive element consists of a tube (made of phosphor bronze), oval in
section, sealed at one end and formed in shape of the greater part of the
circumference of a circle.

• It is roughly the shape of a question mark

• Generally, use for both High- and Low-Pressure Gauges

How does it Work
•
When pressure is applied internally this tube tends to straighten and conversely when
air is exhausted it tends to curl up more sharply.
•
The amount of curvature is approximately proportional to the pressure or vacuum
applied.
•
The mechanism which connects the free end of the bourdon tube to the pointer is
called the movement.
•
Consists of a pivoted toothed quadrant, one end of which is connected by a link to the
end of the tube whilst the teeth mesh with a pinion actuating the pointer.
•
Attached to the pinion is a hair spring, the function of which is to keep the teeth of the
pinion in close contact with those of the quadrant.

---

Fig.7 Bourdon Tube Assembly

Use of Bourdon Tube

•
As the Bourdon tube deflects to the same degree when subjected to negative &
positive pressure, it is not generally used as a compound gauge as the vacuum scale
would be difficult to read with any accuracy.

•
The diaphragm gauge is better suited as a compound gauge as it can be designed to
show greater deflection in its negative scale.

---

Compound Gauge Diaphragm Type
• This type of gauge depends on its operating principle on the movement of a
corrugated diaphragm which is secured at its periphery and is free to move at its
centre.
• It consists of a front and rear housing.
Front housing: small cavity.
Rear housing: large cavity

How does it work?
•
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> The diaphragm is circular in shape and has annular ribs or is corrugated to ensure
flexibility and is secured at its periphery while free to move at its center.
•
The rear housing carrying the screwed connection has a large diameter cavity which
allows the whole of the exposed area of the diaphragm to deflect when subjected to
negative pressure.
•
Upon the application of positive pressure, the deflection of the diaphragm is restricted
to the small cavity in the front housing.

Gauges General
The dials of the high- and low-pressure gauges, along with the compound gauge are usually
filled with a clear viscous gel in an effort to eliminate vibration of the pointer during
pumping operations.

Gauge Cocks
The connections of both the compound and
pressure gauges are usually fitted with gauge
cocks.
These have 2 functions:

1. To shut off the supply to the gauge if the
gauge becomes damaged.
2. To dampen the effects of pulsation in the
pressure being read.

Rev counter

This registers the speed (RPM) of the pump shaft. When priming, the pump operator
uses this gauge to adjust the engine speed via the hand throttle, so as not to exceed
the priming speed of the pump.

Water / Foam level indicator
Registers the quantity of Water/Foam available to the pump operator from the
appliance internal storage tanks. These may be illuminated sight glass tube with hi-
visibility floating ball, graduated from full to empty or illuminated LED display.

Flow meters
Measures the flow rate and total amount of water supplied through deliveries upon
which they are mounted.

Care of Gauges
Gauges are sensitive instruments

•
When opening up or shutting down branches or hydrants, consideration should be
given to the effect of the sudden shock (e.g. water hammer) on the pressure sensitive
elements of the various types of gauges.

•
The sudden application of pressure can cause premature failure of the gauge.

Flow Meters
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Many Fire and Rescue Services are now using fire pumps fitted with flow meters,
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> thus providing the fire fighters with exact information on how much water is being
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> delivered and available. Many of Dublin Fire Brigade’s appliances are fitted with TSI
flow meters which we will discuss in detail in this section.

What is a flow meter and what does it do?
A flow meter is a device
designed to measure the
amount of water passing
at a given point at any
given time. It provides
information on flow in
Litres per Minute (LPM)
but also records the total
volume of water
delivered.
In order to measure individual flow rates from each delivery, the flow meters are
located immediately before the delivery outlet on the pump and are connected to
LCD read outs, which are located within the pump bay at a location to suit individual
FRS requirements.
The main advantages of this type of flow meter are that they are 100% hydraulically
efficient as there is nothing to impede water flow and they are not subject to wear
and tear. This results in extremely accurate flow measurements coupled with an
exceptionally long unit life.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> TSI flow meters are the only flow meters which have no moving parts, do not restrict the
water flow in any way and are accurate to + or – 2%. They have proven longevity and can
provide information, which can be viewed in real time either at the pump or remotely such
as at an Incident Command Unit.

Flow Meter/Sensors

Practical benefits of using Flow instead of Pressure
When pump operators deliver water-
using pressure, even if branch
operators use branches with
adjustable flow rates, the branch
operator may not consistently use the
branch at its maximum setting. If the
branch operator becomes fatigued
over time due to jet reaction, they
may reduce the flow at the branch to
provide some respite. However, this
action may result in insufficient
quantities of water then being
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> delivered onto the fire. The pump
operator would not be aware of any
reduction in the amount of water
being delivered as the pressure gauge
would not register any significant
change.

When using flow meters, any reduction in flow made by the branch operator, or by
debris on hose lines, is reflected in a reduced reading at the flow meter. This enables
the pump operator to communicate with the branch operator for remedial actions
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> to be taken and to ensure fire fighter safety is maintained.

---

Multiple Supplies with Different Flow Rates

Multiple flowrates being supplied from 1 pump via flow meters

When delivering water to multiple branches/equipment, the correct use of flow
meters improves the working conditions for firefighters with the added benefits of
negligible Jet Reaction & the branch receiving its correct flow rate of water.

High Rise Incidents.
Where pumps fitted with flow meters are used to charge rising mains at high-rise
buildings it is clear that the riser is fully charged once the flow reads zero. It is also
obvious when a landing valve is open, as flow will continue to show at the readout
rather than a zero reading being achieved. Equally, when the riser is charged the pump
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> operator can ensure sufficient flow rates are provided to branch operators, which is not
possible when using pressure alone.

---

Portable Flow Meters.

• Multiple flow rates can be supplied safely from a single pump

• Pump Operator is informed when flow stops.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Minimizes hydraulic shock wave when branches are closed
• Better balance of water demand versus supply if metered at pump/tank inlets
• Improves strategic command decision making.
Some examples are:
Better water resource management
Better knowledge of dilution ratio at Chemical Incidents or water load
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> placed on building structure or ballast issues at a ship fire
• 100% hydraulically efficient as there is nothing to impede water flow
•
Restricting the water flow by adjusting delivery valves should only ever be

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> The standard firefighting branches available in Dublin Fire Brigade (excluding

• Fog Branches (Compartment Branches)

Smoothbore Branches
Smoothbore branches are of a “straight through” construction. All smoothbore
branches have the following characteristics:
• Low internal resistance due to straight through construction. No obstruction
to water flow.
• Large flow rates at low branch pressures compared to fog branches.
• They are not easily blocked by debris.
• There is less Jet Reaction for equivalent flow rates compared to Fog Branches.
This results in easier handling and less firefighter fatigue
• Long distance water throw.
• Low maintenance.

Fognail
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Use of the Fognail/Fog Spike provides highly effective fire suppression in confined,
difficult to access areas such as roof spaces. Water is directed at the hottest part of the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
