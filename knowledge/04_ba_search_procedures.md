# BA Search Procedures

*Dublin Fire Brigade — Initial BA Wearers Course*
*Source pages: 43–60*

---

BA Search Procedure
General
The purpose of BA Search Procedures is to facilitate the wearer’s movement
and operations inside a building enabling the BA team to achieve their tasks
effectively and in safety. Without such procedures teams will not be able to
operate in dark, smoky and toxic atmospheres effectively. BA Search
Procedures allow the wearer and their team to achieve the following:
• Protect themselves while they are in the risk area
• Search in a systematic, efficient, effective and safe manner
• Extract themselves at the appropriate time back to the ECP and
minimising the potential for getting lost in a dark and toxic
atmosphere.
The systematic nature of the procedures will help to reduce the likelihood
that the wearer will forget to do something which might result in an
increased risk to the wearers. It is the responsibility of the Team Leader
and individual wearers to follow procedures at all times. Failure to follow
procedures increases the risk of injury and can lead to fatalities.
BA Search Procedures rely on each team member being trained to a very
high level enabling them to operate as an efficient operational unit
working towards a common goal as issued by the Incident Commander.
The essential characteristics of the BA Wearer to ensure efficient search
procedures are as follows
• Self-discipline
• Moral courage
• Training
• Confidence in both set and self
• Ability to cope with mental strain
• Team spirit

---

Over time the experience of the wearer will increase so that many of the
procedures become second nature. It is easy to get lulled into a false
sense of security and become complacent. While conditions may look
like procedures are unnecessary it must always be remembered that
conditions inside the risk area can change rapidly and catch the team off
guard.
The Search Team
The BA search team will consist of a minimum of two (2) and a
maximum of four (4) fire fighters.  One member of the team will be
nominated as the Team Leader by the Incident/sector Commander; this
person will generally be the most experienced wearer and may even be
an officer depending on the task.  The search team may be connected to
each other using personal lines.  The Team Leader will receive their brief
from the Incident/sector Commander and brief the ECO prior to entry
into the risk area. The Team Leader will have communications with the
Entry Control Officer (ECO). The team will only be committed to the
risk area under breathing apparatus entry control rules. Self-deployment
is strictly prohibited.
Moving in the Risk Area
The risk area will frequently be dark and smoky, with personal lamps of
limited value. Therefore, breathing apparatus teams work generally by
touch. In general the team will follow the wall wherever it leads to ensure
that they can follow the same wall back out again.  Moving along the
wall keeps the team in the strongest and generally safest area and avoids
holes in the centre of damaged floors. To protect themselves while
moving the wearer will shuffle and not walk.  This ensures that the
wearer will not walk into obstacles or fall through holes in the floor
which they cannot see. The weight of the body should be placed on the
rear foot until the advancing foot has tested that it is safe to move
forward. The lead foot sweeps low to the ground left to right and also
physical tests the strength of the ground in front of them. If safe to move
forward the weight is transferred onto the front foot and then the rear foot
is brought up to the front foot. The feet should not be lifted from the
ground.  The foot should slide forward as this will help to detect

---

obstructions and dangers such as projecting nails etc. which might pierce
the boots and injure the feet. The front foot is then moved forward again
as the sequence is repeated.
The procedure ensures that if an obstacle or a hole in the floor is
encountered the weight is on the rear foot and the obstacle will not be hit
with force nor should the wearer fall forward into the hole.  As you move
forward one hand should  keep contact with the same wall at all times.
The back of the hand should sweep up and down searching for doors.
The wall should be tapped to determine changes in construction i.e. from
solid block wall to timber door.
It is vital that doors are not missed, as if a door is passed inadvertently
on the way in but found on the way out the team may get lost. The other
hand, slightly clenched, should be raised in front of the face with the
back of the hand facing forwards. The hand is moved slowly up and
down in front of the face to protect the face and air supply from obstacles
/obstructions in front of you. If the back of the hand touches a live electric
cable the shock will throw it clear and will not cause the wearer to grasp
the wire as would occur if the open palm was to touch the cable.
If the team is moving to a specific location, firefighting or returning out,
the remaining team generally tuck in behind the team leader (Path find)
All team members should monitor the wall and be prepared to stop the
team if a door is missed or if there is any uncertainty. When searching
the team fan out to the left or right of the Team Leader who stays on the
wall. As team members are now moving across the centre part of the
floor the shuffle is even more important for their protection.
Fundamentals of Search
When searching for persons, the search should be carried out
methodically using a definite systematic procedure which may be
detailed by the Incident Commander before the task is commenced.

Left / Right Hand Search
The Team Leader will receive the brief as to the task the team is to carry
out from the Incident / Sector Commander.  The team will enter the

---

building on either a left hand search or a  right hand search.  A left-hand
search therefore implies that the Team Leader enters the building with
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> his left hand on the left hand wall.  The Team Leader never takes his
hand off the wall and the team goes wherever the left-hand wall takes
them i.e. through doors up or down stairs/vertical ladders.  Often two
teams will be deployed one to fight / control the fire and the other to
search.  Once a casualty is encountered the priority is to get the casualty
to fresh air as quickly as possible.
Tasks allotted to teams can vary and teams can be directed to specific
locations for specific tasks.  The following are some examples of the
common tasks.
• Fire-fighting search and rescue on the left/right hand wall
• Search and rescue on the left/right hand wall – proceed to the fire
location and search back.
• Search and rescue on the left/right hand wall – search from the
point of entry.
• Search and rescue on a left/right hand wall – proceed to the stairs
and at the top of the stairs search on the left/right hand wall (Note:
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> do not change walls at any point)
• Lay a main guideline on the left/right
• Lay a branch guideline on the left/right off main guideline A/B at
a particular location

Conduct of Search
When teams are searching they must spread out to ensure that as much
off the area as possible is searched.  The Team Leader keeps their hand
on the wall and the remaining team members spread out perpendicular
to the wall along which they are moving and slightly behind the Team
Leader. Each team member must remember to shuffle along the floor and
use their hands to protect themselves from any obstructions at chest/head
level.
The Team Leader is responsible for keeping their team in line but slightly
behind him. In reasonable visibility they may be able to make out their
team members. In complete darkness and smoke they will rely on
reflective background of contents gauges, and the reflective strips of the
individual cylinders. By personal contact the Team Leader can easily
determine the position of the number 2. The Team Leader must
communicate with the remainder of the team mainly telling them of
change of direction, pivot point or end wall. When the Team Leader turns
they should pause and get their team in line again before proceeding.
Individual team members must also communicate certain information
back to the Team Leader in terms of:
•
What they are searching and if extra time is needed
•
When the area is searched and clear and the team can move on
•
Finding a casualty
•
Meeting an opposing wall
•
Monitoring their contents gauge.
Within a three (3) or four (4) person search team the middle personnel
are vital to ensure effective communication between the Team Leader
and the outermost team member. It is important that the casualties in this
space are not missed. Therefore wearers must search to the left and right
side as well as forwards.
The search must be thorough as people will seek refuge from flame and
smoke in unlikely locations such as cupboards and storerooms.  In
particular children have been known to hide under beds, in wardrobes,
in appliances and these must be fully searched. This is particularly
important when the team is searching a room with a lot of furniture or
obstructions and also when the team is changing direction thus ensuring

---

that the team maintains the methodical searching pattern and that the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> remainder of the team do not go ahead of the Team Leader.
When the fire occurs on an intermediate floor of a large building it will
be found in the majority of cases that persons caught above the fire
proceed to the roof level.
Particular attention should be paid to doors or apertures to ensure that
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> you do not pass them without completing the search of each room. If
each room is searched methodically in turn you will thus obtain a clear
indication of the layout of the building.

Door Procedures
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> It is vital that a team searching a building do not miss any doors. Before
opening a door there are specific fire behaviour procedures to be used.
Precautions must also be taken to prevent a door from locking behind the
team (for example if a team made a window entry into an apartment and
then exited the apartment by the front door to search the common
corridor and adjacent apartments).  Locking of the front door (locked out
of the apartment) would deny the team exit by their original entry point.
In order to search the door way properly the following procedure should
be adopted When the Team Leader discovers the door he should inform
the team (The Team Leader stays on the door jamb as his team pivots out
and around search as much of the area outside the door as possible. When
this is finished the Team Leader enters the doorway and moves along the
wall to allow their full team to enter. Once the last firefighter is in the
room the Team Leader tells the team to pivot. The team then backs up to
the maximum extent searching behind the door and then out into the
room in an arc until they are level with the Team Leader who has
maintained position on the door jam.

Passage of Teams
When a team encounters another team, the priority of passage is with the
team on the way out. The incoming Team Leader should gather their
team behind them in a hunkered down position. This allows the exiting
team, who may be under pressure, to follow the wall out unhindered.
When the passage of teams occurs except in exceptional circumstances
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> the two Team Leaders should stop briefly for a short exchange of critical
information. It should first be established that the information from the
exiting team is relevant to the incoming team i.e. they are on the same
wall and are operating in the same location. Key information is generally:
•
Team name
•
Searching right/left
•
Any additional casualties and their locations
•
The extent to which the exiting team has searched
If applicable
•
The location and condition of the fire
The extent of any guidelines laid and location of the bag and spare
bag
•
Any other relevant information for example location of the branch

Route Identification
The Team Leader and team members must memorise the way in by touch
to ensure knowledge of the route.  The Team Leader does this by
identifying reference points along the route to help confirm that the team
is on the correct route on the way out. Landmarks/reference points should
be fixed objects that cannot be moved.  Movable objects may not be in
the same position as they were when the team is returning along a route
leading to confusion and panic. Landmarks/Reference points should be
communicated to the other team members so that they are aware of their
location along a route.  An easily recognisable reference point close to
the exit point will help the team identify that they are nearly out.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Reference points will also help emergency teams locate a team in
distress. It will also help if the Team Leader can build up a mental picture
of the type and sequence of rooms the team pass through. Finally as the
team pass through the building they should protect their escape route by
moving any obstacles in their way which may hinder them on the way
out.

Stairs Procedure
The use of the BA shuffle is vital if the team is to identify a stairs early
and avoid falling down them. Remember buildings and even houses may
have more than one stairs and they can lead down to a basement from
ground floor. When descending stairs it is essential to proceed
backwards, since this shields the face from heat and has the additional
advantage of allowing you grasp the stairs with your hands and so
prevent a fall in case of an accident.  Check the structural stability of
each step before transferring weight onto the step. It is not permitted to
walk down a stairs as you would do normally. Where present and in good
condition always use the handrail for support.
If the wearer descends backwards the hand on the wall must be changed
over. At the bottom of the stairs it must be remembered to change the
correct hand back onto the wall to continue the search Stairwells are
often hot due to the chimney effect and wearers should avoid spending
unnecessary time in stairwells. Where there is doubt about the structural
stability of the stairs it should be descended or ascended one at a time.
Team members should remain in visual or verbal contact while separated
by the stairs
Vertical Ladders
In some commercial premises, BA Teams may encounter vertical ladders
on their route which can cause confusion.  Vertical ladders often serve
mezzanine floors or platforms, confined spaces and are common
throughout ships. Vertical ladders are dealt with in the following manner.
• If searching on the left (i.e. left hand on left hand wall) then
approach the vertical ladder along the left wall
• Ascend or descend the ladder (keeping three points of contact at
all times).
• Step off the ladder on the opposite side to which you are travelling
(i.e. searching on a left hand wall – step off the ladder on the right
side)
• Place your back to the ladder/wall.
• Feel for the wall with your left hand
• Continue your search on the left.

---

Even if the wearer steps off the ladder on the wrong side as a result of an
obstruction or confinement, by placing their back against the wall and
feeling for the wall with the hand on which they are following, they will
still be correct.  Stepping off the ladder on the wrong side however,
increases the risk of a fall to the wearer, particularly having ascended a
vertical ladder, because the wearer will now have to pass over or around
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> the opening in the floor which they just came through. Extreme caution
must be exercised around vertical ladders if a fall is to be avoided, and it
should be remembered that they may be approached without any
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> visibility. Never step off the ladder until you are sure you are stepping
onto solid ground that can support your weight fully.

Vertical ladders tend to become very hot as a result of the chimney effect
and hot metal ladders.  When at the top of a ladder, keep clear of the
opening in the floor as hot air/fire gases will move up through the
opening.  Always check the temperature of the ladder, either with a
Thermal Imaging Camera, the back of the hand or by wetting the ladder
using the hose and observing if the water turns to steam.  If the ladder is
hot it should be cooled using the hose prior to anybody ascending or
descending. Wetting the ladder may cause the ladder to become slippery
temporarily. Minimum time should be spent on the ladder. The longer
the wearer spends on a hot ladder the more likely they are to suffer steam
burns due to damp gloves.

Finally, there should only be one wearer on the ladder at any one time.
When the wearer ascends or descends the ladder he should report the
ladder clear so that their team member can then ascend/descend. Always
move clear of the ladder as serious injury can occur if the wearer on the
ladder was to slip and fall

Contents Gauge Checks
In order to ensure that the BA team exits the risk area before the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> activation of the low pressure warning whistle the Team Leader must
initiate a contents gauge check by each member of the team regularly.
As the rate of air consumption of each wearer is different and is
dependent on physical make up, fitness and work rate of the wearer, there
could be a difference of up to 30 bar in the contents of the wearers’
cylinders.  The decision of the Team Leader to turn around and exit the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> danger area is based on the quantity of air left in the cylinder of the
wearer with the least cylinder contents.
A gauge check of Team Alpha a team of three is as follows;

TL   -
Team Alpha gauge check
No 2  -
Team Alpha gauge check
No 3  -
Team Alpha gauge check (and checks their gauge -
110 bar)

Team Alpha No 3 one ten (110)

No 2  -
Team Alpha No 2 one twenty (120)
TL  -
Team Alpha No 1 ninety (90)

Team turn around and head out of the building

After each contents gauge check the Team Leader must make a decision
as to what to do, continue on, or turn around. The team must be informed
of the decision.
In addition to regular contents gauge checks on route, contents gauge
checks will also be required before ascending or descending a level. They
will also be required when a casualty is located to determine who will
carry what part of the casualty. The principle being the lightest load
should be assigned to the person with the lowest contents gauge reading.
Before entering the building the OIC may indicate a turnaround pressure
an approximate gauge reading which indicates to them that they must
make a decision to either proceed a little more because conditions permit
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> or withdraw and return out.
The TAP is designed to avoid a situation where the Team Leader and the
team become so engrossed in their task that they let their air supply run
dangerously low. If all team members are focussed on the TAP it is a
reading they will look out for. Once the TAP has been reached the Team
Leader will make their decision on what to do. This decision must be
communicated and explained to the team.

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Withdrawal from the Building
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Once the decision is made to withdraw from the building (task complete,
low gauge reading, evacuation or casualty encountered), if the team
enters with the left hand on the left wall, then they leave the building
with the right hand on the same wall. The changing over of hands is a
definite procedure. With the left hand on the left hand wall, place your
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> right hand on top of your left hand. Now withdraw your left hand leaving
the right hand on the wall. Keep the right hand on this wall until you exit
the building. It has been known for wearers to change hand and actually
place their hand on a different wall only to go further into the building
and not out. In this situation the team will become lost.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Lost In Smoke
The possibility of getting lost in a smoky building must always be borne
in mind and a mental note made of any reference points which will assist
in retracing the route to safety.  If the wall is followed correctly then the
only possibility of getting lost is by missing a doorway. Guidelines and
retracing a hose line which has been taken into a building by keeping it
between the feet can also be used to assist in finding the way out, whilst
also staying in contact with the wall.  Hose reels can often end up piled
up inside the building making it difficult to follow, and it should not be
forgotten that a hose line may run over the edge of a flat roof or out of a
window.  It is difficult to identify from a hose reel which way is in and
which is out unless the hose reel is marked.  Hose lines are easier but fire
fighters must be familiar with the direction of the couplings
If a team considers that they are lost but are on a wall (i.e. they have gone
wrong) they should back up and re-trace their steps in contact with the
wall until they reach their last known point.  If a wearer gets separated
or disorientated and lost in a room they should make their way to a wall
and continue round it in the same direction until a door or window is
reached.  Sounds from outside can often provide a guide as to the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> whereabouts of an exit.  If it is suspected that a firefighter is lost in smoke
it may sometimes be possible to indicate to him the whereabouts of the
way out by standing close to it and using a series of hand claps.  It is very
easy to get disorientated in smoke but a distinction should be made
between domestic houses and large more complicated and larger
buildings such as factories or department stores.  It might be reasonable

---

to assume a reasonable chance of reaching a window in the former and
visibility might be better in the latter.
On certain floors in some buildings (particularly the basements of large
stores) there may be a central staircase only.  If a circuit of the walls has
been made without a way out being found, the centre should be explored.
In thick smoke mirrors may look like windows or may reflect the fire
whilst retail showcases may also in some instances give the same
impression.  If the way out cannot be found, care must be taken not to
stray too far from the original route so as to avoid increasing the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> difficulty for any following emergency teams.  Consideration should
strongly be given to adopting the entrapped procedure and waiting for
rescue (see section 7.0).
Basic Deployment Principles
On arrival at an incident were BA is likely to be used the following
guiding principles should be used:
• Contact a person with “Local Knowledge” and gain as much
information about the premises as possible.  Ask if there are plans
of the building available or consult pre-incident plans.
• Where persons are reported try and get their last known location
• Formulate a plan and keep to it as much as possible.  It should be
noted however, that as the situation within the risk area changes the
plan may also need to change.
• Explain to your crew and perhaps a responsible person attached to
the premises the plan formulated.
• Check the air supply before entering.
• Operate Stage I or Stage II entry control procedure as appropriate.
• Guide lines, hose reel or a line of hose can be used as a guide back
to the entry point with certain precautions, such as maintaining
contact with the wall.
Remember BA teams are vulnerable.  Other actions may be necessary to
assist in their protection. On the other hand inadvertent or unplanned
actions by others even in areas remote from the BA team may have
serious consequences for the team.  Unauthorised actions (including
ventilation) or self-deployment are strictly prohibited.  All actions must
be sanctioned by the Incident / Sector Commander.

Managing Hose
When a hose line is brought into the building it must be managed or it
will snag and delay and disrupt the team. For anything other than short
or uncomplicated runs, pulling of the hose reel or line by the team at the
branch will be difficult and they will need support. Working hose can
use up a considerable amount of energy and air. Procedures for hose reel
are as follows:
• Snake out sufficient hose reel outside the building. If necessary
extend the hose reel
• Test the branch
• The team takes the hose reel into the building assisted by
personnel feeding in the hose reel at the entry point
• If the entry is to be a long way from the entry point or a
complicated run,  a second team may be deployed to work along the
hose reel inside the building ensuring an adequate supply for the
team at the branch
• Another option is for the hose reel to be coiled and coils brought
in on the shoulder of each team member. As the team progresses the
hose reel is deployed from the shoulder initially by the rear most
member of the team. This technique is difficult to manage when the
team have other things to do such as door procedures, negotiating
stairs etc.

When hose line (i.e. 51mm or 70mm delivery hose) is being deployed
the work rate will generally be much higher. The procedure is as follows:
• Snake out sufficient hose outside the building
• Test the branch
• Two two-man teams are necessary. One team at the branch and
one team to work along the route of the hose to ensure an adequate
supply of hose to the team at the branch.
• The hose should be fed into the building by other personnel at the
entry point

Casualty Handling Procedures
Casualty handling techniques are important in a BA environment
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> because it is important that a casualty is removed from the danger area
without causing further injury to the casualty or causing injury to the BA
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Wearers.  When manually handling a casualty from the danger it is
important for the BA Team Leader and wearers to consider the
following:
• Task:  Is manual handling the casualty necessary?  Can the
casualty move themselves?
• Is there equipment readily available to assist the wearers and the
casualty?
• Load: How heavy is the Casualty?  Will the move aggravate any
injuries?
• Rescuers: Are you and the Team Members properly trained?  Is
the task within your capabilities?
The urgency of the rescue is also a factor in the Team Leader’s decision
as to which type of casualty handling technique is to be used in the
removal of the casualty from the risk area.  If the casualty’s condition is
such that they requires urgent removal from the risk area to prevent
further deterioration then obviously the team will employ casualty
handling techniques that will get the casualty as quickly as possible out
of the risk area.  There are a number of techniques available to the BA
wearer for removing a casualty from the risk area.

Wearers should also remember the basic rules of manual handling as
follows:
• Firm and level base with feet shoulder width apart
• Take a firm grip
• Bend the knees and keep the back straight but not overly straight
• Avoid twisting the torso.

The Blanket Technique
A blanket or salvage sheet can be used to assist with the removal of a
casualty over a smooth surface. This technique is particularly useful for
removing large casualties or casualties requiring careful removal.  If the
casualty is in a toxic atmosphere this technique will not be suitable as the
casualty will be having difficulty breathing and as the team will not be

---

carrying a salvage sheet or blanket they will have to wait for another
team to come in with a sheet. An alternative to the salvage sheet is the
sked stretcher. Both wearers should use the BA shuffle to protect
themselves and the number 1 must keep contact with the wall.
Two Person Drag
This is a commonly used method of casualty removal particularly when
teams are under pressure. The casualty is dragged by the arms whilst
their head is lifted clear of the floor to prevent serious injury.  In
Breathing Apparatus the Team Leader can still follow the designated
wall and personal protection is possible.  Personal Lines can be
disconnected when handling casualties as the lines can cause
entanglement and the casualty creates a connection between the wearers.

The Stairs Technique
Method 1 (Descending)
If stairs are encountered along the escape route, the team member will
descend backwards with the casualty head first supported under the arms.
This is to ensure the safety of both the rescuer and the casualty.  The
Team Leader / number 1 will check the safety of the stairs and provide
additional support to the back of the team member supporting the
casualty.  This method of taking a casualty is particularly suitable for
lighter casualties

Method 2 (Descending)
For heavier casualties. This method of moving a casualty down a stairs
could be considered and may be more appropriate if the casualty is very
heavy or the fire fighter is fatigued due to the amount of work conducted.
This technique entails the No 1 (in a two man team) or the No 2 (in a
three man team) turning around, holding the casualties legs under his arm
and progressing down the stairs backwards, making sure to maintain
contact with the wall with the appropriate arm.  No 2 (in a two man team)
or No 3 ( in a three man team) supports the casualty’s torso under the

---

arms, sits on the steps and progresses down the stairs step by step
lowering the casualty onto each step as they progress.  The disadvantage
with this method of moving a casualty down a stairs compared to the
method outlined above is that it is considerably slower and in a smoky
atmosphere, it may take longer to get the casualty to fresh air.

In deciding on the most appropriate method of carrying a casualty down
a stairs the team leader must first conduct a dynamic risk assessment
taking into account the weight of the casualty, the condition of the
casualty, distance to safe air, the structural stability of the stairs the
condition of the BA team and any other factors that the team leader feels
may affect the casualty’s chances of survival and the teams safety.
Ascending a Stairs
When carrying a casualty up a stairs the most appropriate method is the
reverse of Method 2 outlined above. As stated in the paragraph above in
deciding on the most appropriate method of carrying a casualty up a
stairs the team leader must first conduct a dynamic risk assessment
taking into account the weight of the casualty, the condition of the
casualty, distance to safe air, the structural stability of the stairs, the
condition of the BA team and any other factors that the team leader feels
may affect the casualty’s chances of survival and the teams safety.  Team
leaders should note that the strongest part of a stairs is the area where the
steps are attached to the wall.  Using the two person drag on a stairs
involves the two fire fighters occupying the same step thereby increasing
the weight on any one step and also involves one fire fighter being further
from the wall (the weaker part of the step).
Two Person Carry
For light casualties it may be possible to execute a two-person carry. The
wearer with the highest gauge reading grabs the casualty by the torso and
the wearer with the lowest gauge reading takes the legs under the arm
not against the wall.  Personnel protection is done by the wall hand but
keeping the shoulder against the wall. Where guide lines are in use, it
may be easier to place the casualty under the arm closest to the wall.

Spinal Care
For a casualty with suspected spinal injuries it may be possible to
implement standard casualty handling techniques including C spine
immobilisation and spinal boarding.  In most situations this will not be
feasible, however if time was available and the casualty was breathing
the casualty could be fitted with a facemask (connected to an airline or
from a rescue set) and supplementary supply. Where the casualty is not
breathing or suspected of not breathing it is imperative that the casualty
be removed to fresh air as quickly as possible.

Casualty Handling on Vertical Ladders
Lifting or lowering a casualty up or down a vertical opening is very
difficult and alternate approaches should always be considered. In
training, casualties are lowered and raised using a drop line tied in a
bowline around the torso of the casualty. The casualty is then hauled or
lowered by a number of personnel. Where a dedicated hauling system is
available and persons are trained in its use this should be considered.

Care of Property
BA teams have to operate by touch in difficult conditions without
visibility.  Care should be taken in the way teams deal with the property
of others including the movement of furniture.  Where a room is in
disarray this will be very obvious once the fire is out and smoke has been
cleared.  Every effort, within reason, should be taken to care properly for
the property of others.
