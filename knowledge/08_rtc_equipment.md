Section 2: RTC Equipment
2.1 Introduction
The correct operation and maintenance of RTC equipment is essential during any RTC
incident. As there is considerable physical effort required when using rescue tools, and many
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> tools are heavy, correct manual handling techniques must be always used. Appropriate PPE
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> must be always worn. When using tools, the operators should be alert to the danger posed
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> by Supplemental Restraint Systems (SRS), airbags, pre-tensioners etc. Trim or other linings
should be removed to enable any potential hazards to be seen.  All RTC equipment is to be
used in conjunction with the manufacture’s guidelines and current Dublin Fire Brigade SOG’s.
2.2 Safety while using rescue equipment
•
Equipment must only be used by trained personnel.
•
Only use the tool for its intended purpose.
•
Both inner and outer visors must be down.
•
Gloves must be worn.
•
Dust masks must be in place if glass is being cut or broken.
•
Remove all interior plastics and coverings before making any cuts.
•
Continuously check vehicle stabilization.
•
When a tool is used to lift a load, it must be backed up with cribbing/chocks.
•
Keep inserting cribbing/chocks as the lifting height increases.
•
If any tool must be used, hard protection must be in place for both the casualty and
the users.
•
Battery operated tools are a two-person operation. (Lifting and/or hard protection)
•
The tool casings are plastic therefore it is imperative that the rear of the tool does not
come under pressure from being wedged against any parts of the vehicle.
•
Tools are not to be used as battering rams.
•
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Do not stand between the tool and Vehicle when using the tools.
•
Inspect all tools/batteries and ancillary equipment for damage before and after use.
•
All RTC procedures must be adhered to.
•
Long sleeved Hi-Viz must be worn.

2.4 Lukas Battery Rescue Tools
2.4.1 Battery Cutters
Cutters are more important on the rescue scene than ever before. This is due in part to
advances in vehicle design and construction and improvements in cutter technology. The
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> enormous power available in these tools allows for a significant cutting or crushing hazard
when used carelessly.

Points to remember:
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Remove all plastic or other interior trim in vehicles before cutting to check for airbag
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> inflators, SRS systems or any other potential hazards.
• Position the cutter so that it is at a 90-degree angle to the cutting surface.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Ensure the material to be cut is as far into the blade recess as possible. Avoid cutting
at the tips.
• If the cutter is twisting and the blades begin to separate, stop cutting and reposition.
• Avoid cutting unsecured objects.
• Beware of cutting hardened steel or gas struts.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Do not try to stop the natural movement of the tool, stop and reposition the tool or
yourself before becoming trapped against the vehicle.

2.4.2 Battery Spreaders
The spreader is a powerful tool and when used effectively and can be very efficient in the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> extrication process. Spreaders represent a significant crush hazard when used carelessly.

The following points should be noted when using a spreader. The main aspect to consider is correct
placement on a stable spreading platform. Once the tool begins to spread it supports most or all of its
own weight so that from this point on it is only necessary to support the tool and operate the control
handle.
(A guide to rescue tool handling and extrication techniques, Holmatro, 2013)

Points to remember:
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> - Try to always use the full surface of the spreading tips.
- Should the tips start to lose their grip, stop and reposition.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> - Ensure that the tool position is such that material is pushed to the outside of the vehicle.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> - You cannot prevent the natural movement of a tool during operation. Ensure that you stop
and reposition before the tool or parts of your body become trapped against components of
the vehicle.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> - Never place your hands on the arms or tips of the spreader.
- After working with the spreader, it is important to place the tool in its “safe” position

2.4.3 Battery Rams
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Rams are an essential part of the rescue battery tool set. It may not always be necessary to
make use of them in every case as with a cutter and spreader, but they are invaluable in
situations where the front of the vehicle is causing entrapment of the occupants. Due to the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> loads that rams can push and hold, sudden kicking out due to loss of grip is the primary hazard.
Points to remember:
• Beware of sudden kicking out due to loss of grip.
• Use sill and other supports where appropriate.
• Position the ram so the control handle is accessible and that it will not get in the way
of the extrication.
• Position the ram such that, if the control handle moves, it will not trap the user’s
hands.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • If ramming is interrupted, be careful of the handle orientation; do not accidentally
begin to lower or release pressure on the ram.
• Pay attention to both purchase points.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Provide stabilisation below the lower purchase point before applying pressure.
• Chock and block as you go.

The winch allows one person to move very heavy objects, up to several tonnes in weight.  A
Tirfor Winch uses a self-gripping jaw (rather than a spool on a conventional winch) to move
wire rope through the winch.  The "jaw" exerts a grip on the wire rope in proportion to the
load being lifted or pulled.  The Safe Working Load (S.W.L) is stamped on the side of the unit
with its own independent number for identification purposes.

---

Operation of the Tifor Winch
The Tirfor Winch is one person operated and under no circumstances should the telescopic
handle, supplied with the unit, be added on to in any way as this can lead to the S.W.L. being
contravened, but more importantly, may result in damaging the machine.
An operating lever fitted to either the forward or reverse lever transmits the effort to the jaw
mechanism to give forward or reverse movement of the wire rope.  The tirfor winch in use in
DFB is hand operated.  The machine is fitted with an anchor pin so that it can be secured
quickly to any suitable anchor point. The Tirfor has a "winching in lever", which brings in the
load, a "reversing handle", which releases the load and a "release lever".  By catching the
reversing lever and the release lever together and moving them forward, the machine will be
in neutral thus making it possible to pay the rope through the unit and under the anchor pin.
The casing is in two halves made of aluminium and held together by Allen screws.

Releasing the wire rope
It’s essential to take the load off the machine before attempting to release the jaws!
• Operate the reverse operating lever until there is no tension on the wire rope
• Release the machine and follow the instructions for installing the wire rope in the
reverse order.
• Re-engage the jaws of the winch before storage.
Care and Maintenance
As previously stated, the Tirfor Winch must be certified on being taken into service. This
certificate is issued by the suppliers who are recognised agents for such equipment.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Never use a machine which is not in good working condition
• Store the winch and wire rope in a dry place, out of the effects of weather.
• The wire rope should be completely removed from the winch before storage.
• Before storing the wire rope it should be examined for fraying or damage to wires.
Signs of wear should be reported to your officer.
• The rope should be cleaned with a brush if needed.
• The winch can be washed in a bath of proprietary cleaner - not acetone or acetone
derivatives nor ethylene trichorlide. The winch can then be shaken vigorously to
loosen any foreign matter and turned upside down to allow dirt to come out the
openings of the operating levers. Allow winch to drain dry.
• After washing, the winch should be lubricated with oil (type SAE 90-120) on the
internal mechanism through the openings for the operating levers.
Winching Techniques
To remove a casualty (vehicle) the OIC must consider four things:
• The Rolling Resistance i.e. the nature of the ground and the weight of the vehicle.
• The Gradient Resistance i.e. the slope of the ground.
• The Damage Resistance i.e. a flat type or a wheel off or maybe brakes stuck on.
• The Ground Factor i.e. is it on a hard surface, is it on grass or sand etc.

---

Rolling Resistance Formula

To calculate the gradient resistance of a 10-ton vehicle up to 45 degrees of slope the
formula is:

For vehicles with a gradient of more than 45 degrees use the weight of the vehicle plus the
Rolling Resistance. Therefore, the estimated pull of a 10-ton vehicle over 45 degrees of slope
would be:
= R.R. + Weight of Vehicle
= 5 + 10 = 15 tons
If a vehicle is on its side and you want to turn it over again you use 2/3rd. of the weight of the
vehicle.
If you wish to turn a vehicle end over end you use 11/4 times the weight of the vehicle.

Air Mat Operational Procedures:
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Ensure the hand wheel/valve is the closed /off position
• Open cylinder valve/check contents
• Connect pressure reducer air supply hose to controller (push nipple into coupling
until it catches.)
• Equipment should only be used by trained personnel.
• Full personal protective equipment. (P.P.E.) Helmet firefighting tunic pull-ups boots
gloves and goggles must be worn at all times
• All equipment must be in a serviceable condition.
• At least 75% of mat should be under load.
• Select the appropriate mat for the load to be lifted.
• Secure load against slipping
• Avoid angles larger than 30 degrees (relationship between base and load to be lifted)
• Keep inserting props/chocks the higher the lifting height.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • If a substructure is required   always ensure that it is stable and firm. That it supports
at least the complete area of the mat and should be wider and longer than it is
high.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • In operation, never stand in front of, but always to the side of the mat.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> • Never stand in front of mat in use as it can be catapulted out from under load.
• Two mats at most may be stacked atop each other.
• Mats of the same size must be stacked exactly atop each other
• With mats of different sizes, the smaller mat must be positioned centrally atop the
larger mat
• To increase lifting power use mats in tandem (side by side)
• Avoid hot exhaust
• Avoid sharp objects
