Introduction
A pump is a machine, driven by external power, for transmitting energy to fluids. This power
may be provided manually by the operator’s own effort (i.e. as in hand pumps) or by coupling
the pump to a suitable engine or motor. This section deals mainly with the latter although it
deals with the general principles of all pumps.

Although there are many different types of pumps, one in particular has come to be regarded
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> in this country as the best for Fire Brigade work. This is the Centrifugal pump and variations
thereof known as a Peripheral or Regenerative pumps. The principles upon which all pumps
operate have been known for centuries.

On the first stroke the plunger (a) moves upward, the inlet valve (b) opens, and a quantity of

Another method of providing a more continuous flow of water from a single acting pump,
which may also be used in conjunction with a double-acting pump as an additional refinement
is the use of an air vessel. (See Fig. 4.).

This is an airtight chamber connected to the delivery side of the pump. When pumping begins
this chamber contains only air at atmospheric pressure. As water begins to flow from the
outlet, some of it enters the chamber and the air, being above the liquid, is compressed.
Therefore, the air in the chamber is now at a higher pressure than that of the atmosphere and
has a tendency to drive the water out whenever there is any slackening of the positive
pressure exerted by the pump, thus maintaining a steady flow.

Bucket and Plunger Pumps
This pump is a combination of both force and lift pumps, consisting of a cylinder with an inlet
valve and a hollow plunger similar to that of a lift pump. The plunger is mounted on a trunk or
column which works through a stuffing box in the same way as the normal plunger does

---

When the plunger (a) rises it draws in water through the inlet valve (b) at the same time any
water which has passed through the plunger valve (c) on the previous stroke is lifted by the
plunger, the valve (c) remains closed. A portion of the water is forced out through the outlet
(d) but some remain in the upper part of the cylinder. When the plunger descends, water is
forced out by the action of the trunk or column (e) in displacing it to make room for its own
bulk. At the same time the valve (c) opens, and the water, which was drawn into the lower
part of the cylinder on, the previous stroke passes through into the upper part. A continuous
action is thus achieved although the pump is not double action.

This pump has many applications in ground use, the best known of which is the stirrup hand
pump which is used predominantly for extinguishing chimney fires.

Rotary Pumps
These pumps differ from the 3 previous types in that although they are also positive
displacement pumps, their action is continuous instead of intermittent or pulsating.

The gear pump is a type of rotary pump widely used in all circulating systems and for pumping
relatively small volumes of water.

Fig. 7. Shows a Rotary Gear type pump

A portion of the fluid drawn into the pump is carried back into the inlet between the teeth.

Introduction

plunger to create a pressure differential but either a liquid or a gas can be employed as the
operating medium or propellant as it is generally termed using the venture principle.

Construction

Ejector pumps used for firefighting consist of a metal body having a water inlet to supply the
jet, a suction inlet, and a delivery outlet. Water is used as a propellant, which is usually
supplied under pressure from a conventional pump (called a primary pump when used in this
way)

The water is delivered from the pump through a hose line to the nozzle of the ejector pump.
With the reduction of pressure that takes place at this point, the water it is intended to
remove, is forced by atmospheric pressure to rise in the suction hose until it is caught up in
the jet stream and passed to the delivery side of the pump, from which it is delivered through
hose to the discharge point.

Centrifugal Pumps
Introduction
Centrifugal pumps are the most widely used for Firefighting. The centrifugal pump has no
valves, pistons or plungers, does not work by displacement and is therefore unable to pump
gases. It makes use instead of centrifugal force (i.e. the force which a rotating body experiences
tending to make it fly away from the axis of rotation). It consists of a spinning circular metal
casting with several radial vanes known as an impeller. Enclosed in a casing, water is received
at its centre and discharged at its periphery. All Centrifugal Pumps used for Firefighting must
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> adhere to I.S. EN 1028-1:2002+A1:2008 Fire-fighting pumps - Fire-fighting centrifugal pumps
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> with primer - Part 1: Classification - General and safety requirements

The impeller, which is rigidly mounted inside a casing on a central shaft driven by external
power, is rotated at the speed required to produce the necessary pressure, thus inducing a flow
through the pump. Water from the suction inlet is picked up by the impeller vanes, which pass it
through to the periphery, its speed obviously increased as it nears the impeller rim, where it is
thrown off.

The water at this point is discharged at such high velocity that the greater part of its energy is
in the form of “Kinetic” or “Velocity” energy, but it now enters the casing where the velocity is
greatly reduced and most of its energy is converted into the form required, “Pressure” energy.

The construction of the centrifugal pump is relatively simple; it consists of two main parts.

• The Impeller imparts high velocity to the water.

• The Casing to transform the velocity energy into pressure energy.

---

The Impeller
Rotation of the circular metal impeller (Fig. 1. below) causes water to be discharged from the
periphery at high velocity. This induces a flow through the impeller passages and causes a
partial vacuum to be created at its inlet.

Fig.1. The construction of a typical impeller.

The Casing
The important function of the casing is to convert the Kinetic energy of the water leaving the
impeller into Pressure energy; this is done by reducing the velocity of the water. Inevitably
there is considerable turbulence in the water as it is discharged at high speed from the
impeller. Turbulence and friction both represent wasted energy, and it is important to keep
these to a minimum. There are two ways of achieving this, by the Volute or by Guide Vanes.

The Volute
The Volute is the simplest type of centrifugal pump casing. It is shaped rather like the shell of a
snail and the water thrown from the impeller enters a passage, which steadily increases in
cross sectional area until it reaches the delivery outlet. By this means the velocity of the water
is gradually reduced.

---

The Volute has three main tasks.
• To channel water from the periphery of the impeller to the outlet.
• To reduce the velocity of the water as it passes through.
• To reduce turbulence of the water as it leaves the impeller.

Cavitation:
When pumping from an open supply, the pressure on the inlet side of the pump can be so
low that the water can vaporise at ambient temperature. Vapour bubbles then form (this is
known as cavitation) and the pump gives off a distinctive rattling sound. This will indicate to
the operator that the maximum flow condition has been reached and should ease back the
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> throttle until the noise disappears. Always warn branch operators of a change in supply
conditions.

The vacuum gauge gives no indication that cavitation conditions are reached because it registers
the general suction condition in the suction inlet of the pump and is not affected by the low-
pressure pockets which form at the entry to the impeller and the back surfaces of the impeller blades.

Multi-Stage and Peripheral Pumps
Introduction
Pumps with a single impeller are capable of developing pressures of anything up to about 20
bar depending on the particular design and the flowrate required (World Series and Prima 17
Bar). If higher pressures are required, for the operation of high-pressure hose reels for example,
there are two methods by which they might be achieved with a single impeller of this type:
(i)
By increasing the speed of the impeller
(i)
By increasing its diameter
Increasing the speed of the impeller can only be achieved by increasing the speed of the engine
and it may be neither practicable nor desirable to do this. Increasing the diameter is
comparatively inefficient and would make the pump bulkier. Hence, to achieve high outlet
pressure, it is better to use a multi-stage pump i.e. a pump with two or more impellers in series.
The impellers are driven by the same rotating shaft with water fed from the periphery of the
first impeller to the entry to the second etc., so that neglecting friction losses, the pressure
increasing ability of the centrifugal pump is applied several times. If the impellers are of the type
already described, then several stages are required to achieve the high pressures needed for
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> hose reel operation and although pumps of this type are in use, most fire service pumps consist
of only two stages and use a different type of impeller for the second stage. This type of impeller
is called the regenerative or peripheral type.
Practical multi-stage pumps have the important advantage that water may be discharged at
relatively low pressure after passing through the first stage only(17bar), or at high pressure (up
to about 55 bar) after passing through subsequent stages. It is also possible to deliver water at
both high and low pressure simultaneously.
-

-

-

Vehicle mounted pumps are normally two-stage and designed to be driven through the power
take-off of the engine of a stationery appliance. The speed at which the pump can run is,
therefore, conditioned by the capability of the engine from which it is taking its power. Pumps
designed for high speeds, however, may have a speed-increasing gear built into them or at the
power take-off.

Portable pumps are invariably single stage.

---

Peripheral (Regenerative) Pumps:
A two-stage pump consisting of two identical impellers would develop a second stage working
pressure of approximately twice that of the first stage and for that second stage pressure to be
high enough for hose reel operations, very high engine speeds would be required. This in turn
would mean that the first stage pressure could be dangerously high for use with normal low
pressure firefighting hose and branches.

In order to achieve a high second stage pressure at a
moderate pump speed a peripheral or regenerative impeller
is employed.

The benefits of a Peripheral Pump are:

• Saves on wear and tear of moving parts
• Allows high pressure delivery at lower engine speeds

Fig 1. Show the operating Principle
of the Peripheral Pump.

This pump develops output pressures which are three or four times as great as those
developed by a conventional centrifugal impeller of equal diameter, run at the same speed.

Fig 2. Shows the regenerative pressure accumulating as the water passes through a peripheral
impeller from inlet increasing 4 times

It is ideally suited for pumping the relatively small quantities of water which are required for
hose reel applications.
Its impeller has at its periphery, a ring of guide vanes, (See image on previous page) the casing fits
closely around the central part of the impeller but leaves a channel around the part containing
the vanes.

Water enters this channel via an inlet, drops to the base of the guide vanes and is then thrown
outwards between the vanes by centrifugal force. It then moves to the base of the vanes again

---

and the process is repeated.

This happens many times while the water is dragged round in a circle by the rotating impeller
until it finally reaches the outlet.

The water therefore follows a spiral path around the channel. The cumulative effect is to
develop pressure equivalent to that of a multi-stage pump but at revolutions consistently below
3000 rpm. Peripheral pumps are particularly suitable for high-pressure hose-reel use to produce
water-fog.

Pumps with Two or More Stages
When the speed of the centrifugal pump is increased, not only does the velocity of the water at
the impeller periphery increase, with a consequent rise in pressure, but also the velocity of water
through the impeller passages, with a consequent rise in quantity
At a constant engine speed, the larger the diameter of the impeller, the greater the speed of its
periphery will be.

The pressure and quantity of water given by a pump is therefore governed by:

• The speed.
• The diameter and size of the passages of the impeller.
Large diameter impellers are comparatively inefficient and where, owing to engine design, the
speed at which the impeller can be run is limited, or it is desired to keep pump speed
comparatively low, it is better to have more than one impeller with moderate diameter ratios
than a single impeller with a disproportionate ratio.

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> In such cases more than one stage is used, although in fire pumps the number of stages does
not normally exceed two.

Two stage pumps
The World Series/Prima pumps can be single or two stage pumps. The first stage of the
two-stage pump has a conventional centrifugal impeller and the second stage uses a
peripheral or regenerative impeller.

Speed and output limits
The pump speed should be limited so that the maximum low pressure attainable is limited
to 17 bar.

The pressure generated in the high-pressure side of the pump is internally limited not to
exceed 55 bar.

Priming speed should be limited to 2,500 RPM, however with piston primers priming will
occur at idle speed although this will take longer.
The minimum idle speed should be at least 1,200 RPM to prevent the piston primers re-
engaging and working continuously (automatic primers only). This will reduce wear and
tear on the piston primer components.

Pump Operation Principle

The Godiva WT & P2 two stage dual pressure pumps can be used for:
-
High volume at low pressure
-
Low volume at high pressure
-
High and low pressure simultaneously
-
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Whilst the pump is running in low pressure mode, low pressure flow is always available
from all outlets including the hose reels (the lever is to the right in the “low pressure”
mode). High Pressure is only available from the hose reels when the high-pressure selector
valve is closed (lever moved to the left in the “high pressure” mode).
Low Pressure Mode
To supply high volume from the delivery valves (and low volume at low pressure from the
hose reels simultaneously) the pressure selector valve must be in the Low-Pressure
position. The pressure developed by the centrifugal impeller will be related to the speed
of the pump shaft and the quantity of the water being discharged. Water will be passed
through the inter stage filter to the high-pressure impeller and flow from this impeller will
be diverted back into the inter stage filter chamber. This then equalizes inlet to outlet
pressures from this impeller. The re-circulated water is then directed at the high-pressure
filter helping to dislodge accumulated debris.
High Pressure Mode
To supply high volume at low pressure from the delivery valves and simultaneously supply
low volume at high pressure from the hose reels the pressure selector valve should be in
the High-Pressure position. The pressure that is developed in the high-pressure stage is
approximately 4 times that of the low pressure and it is now prevented from equalizing
because of the pressure selector valve position. With the valve closed, a resistance to
flow is created and high pressure is generated.
If the high-pressure stage produces excess pressure the pressure relief valve starts to open
allowing water to return to the inter stage filter chamber. As previously stated, this limits
high pressure to a maximum of 55 bar.

Introduction

Priming Systems
Before a centrifugal pump can be got to work it must be primed, i.e. enough air must be
exhausted from the pump casing and suction to allow atmospheric pressure to force water
up into them.
Priming is only necessary with Centrifugal pumps. Positive displacement pumps, i.e. Force,
Lift, Bucket and Plunger and Rotary pumps are themselves capable of exhausting air from the
suction and so have no need for a supplementary primer. They are frequently described as
self-priming pumps for this reason. Centrifugal pumps cannot pump air and are not therefore
self-priming.
There are three ways of priming a centrifugal pump.
• By gravity (Fig.1)
• By Pressure-fed supply eg: a hydrant (Fig.2).
• By use of a primer (Fig.3)

Reciprocating Piston Primers

A reciprocating piston primer consists of a small piston which is driven by an eccentric cam on
the main pump drive shaft. On the induction stroke a vacuum is created in the priming valve
body. Atmospheric pressure causes the
automatic priming valve to open and air is
drawn by the piston through the inlet flap
valves from the suction tube. On the exhaust
stroke air in the primer is forced out through
the exhaust flap valves to atmosphere. Each
primer will discharge approximately 1 litre of
water during a complete priming operation and
a characteristic “popping” noise will be heard.
Once a pump pressure of (>1.5bar World
Series, >0.75bar Prima) is generated it is
communicated to the back of the piston which
lifts off the cam so that the primer effectively
disengages. Figure 5 shows this process in
operation.

Exhaust Gas Ejector Primer

Fig. 6. This type of primer is operated by the exhaust gases from the engine.
With the engine running and the pump primed, the engine exhaust gases pass from the
exhaust manifold, through the ejector housing and into the silencer in the normal way (Fig. 4.
top). The ejector becomes operational when the primer rod (1) is pulled to the limit of its
travel (Fig. 4. bottom).
The action of pulling the rod closes the butterfly valve (2) in the ejector housing (3) which
then causes the exhaust to be deflected through the ejector nozzle (4) thereby creating a
vacuum in the priming pipe (5) and on the outlet side of the non-return valve (6). This causes
the non-return valve to lift off its seating against the pressure of its spring (7). It also draws
the diaphragm (8) down, which moves the sealing washer (9) against the pressure of the
spring (10) into the open position. Depression then allows water to flow into the volute under
atmospheric pressure.

---

As the pump is primed, evacuated air and then water pass through the system and into the
silencer (11).

When the pump priming is complete (indicated by the first positive needle movement of the
pump pressure gauge) the priming rod (1) is released and returns to its static position under
the influence of a return spring, causing the butterfly valve (2) to open. The exhaust gases
then resume their normal path (Fig. 4. top), the depression in the priming pipe (5) and outlet
housing side of the non-return valve (6) is destroyed and the valve closes under the influence
if its spring (7). The diaphragm (8) returns to its static position under the influence of the
spring (10) and the sealing washer (9) contacts its seating.
Some modern pumps have an automatic priming valve so that only the exhaust butterfly
needs to be closed to prime the pump.
With this type of primer, efficiency depends on the speed at which the gases leave the ejector
nozzle. Priming is, therefore, carried out at high engine revolutions i.e. full throttle. The device
is extremely simple and effective in operation and is less subject to damage from mishandling
than other types, however, it requires more skill in manipulation on the part of the operator,
particularly on deep lifts, than is necessary with the reciprocating primer.
The Godiva 8/5 Power flow light portable pump has a manual priming valve which must be
manually opened as well as pulling the priming lever.

Rotary Water Ring Primers

Water Ring Primers
Fig. 7. Shows a Water Ring Primer.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> A water ring primer is a form of positive displacement pump. It is widely used in the Fire
Service and is engaged and disengaged either manually or automatically.
The principle of operation is very simple (Fig. 5.). A vaned impeller with a hollow centre
rotates in an elliptical housing around a stationary hollow boss, which is a projection from the
housing end cover. This boss has four ports in its periphery, which communicates with the
primer suction and delivery connections.
When priming commences and the impeller rotates, the liquid in the housing is compelled by
centrifugal force to move outwards and follow the contour of the housing, thus forming a
hollow elliptical vortex. This liquid “Ring” rotates in the housing with the impeller and as it
rotates from the minor diameter of the ellipse towards the major diameter it moves radially
outwards between the impeller vanes. After it passes the major diameter and rotates towards
the minor diameter it moves radially inwards. As the liquid moves radially outward between
the vanes air is drawn into the impeller through ports in the centre, which communicate with
the suction ports in the central stationary boss and so with the pump suction line. As the liquid
moves inward this air is forced through the impeller ports into the “discharge” in the central
boss.
Since the impeller is located centrally in the elliptical housing there are two pumping actions
in each revolution.
The appropriate engine speed for the operation of water ring primers is about 2500 rpm.

B. Use of a Blank Cap
