Fundamentals of Fire and Heat Transfer
Combustion
Combustion is defined as:
A chemical reaction where oxygen combines (reacts) with other substances
(fuel) and in the presence of an ignition source (heat) produces heat and
light.
Combustion is often represented by the fire triangle, depicting the three
ingredients necessary in order for combustion to occur.

Flame
Flame is the region in which the chemical reaction (combustion) takes
place and where unburned fuel gases are converted into heat, light and
combustion product gases. There are two flame types as follows:
•
Pre-mixed flame, and
Diffusion flame

To understand the difference we need to look at laminar and turbulent
flows. Laminar flow is characterised by a very smooth flow. In a turbulent
flow the smooth flow is broken up and becomes very fragmented. Both
types of flow are shown in Figure 1.2.

Diffusion flames are turbulent flames such as those of a fire in a domestic
fire grate or the burning of rubbish. In this type of flame the air and the
fuel are mixed in the combustion zone (the flame region). Laminar flow
on the other hand is typically associated with the burning of gases such as
a Bunsen burner in a school laboratory or a gas cooker. In this flame type

Pyrolysis
In combustion of gases, the gas is in the form that can burn. For liquids
and solid materials it is not the liquid or solid that burns but flammable
vapours released just above the material. For a solid to burn it must be
heated. The heat releases the flammable vapours which then burn just
above the solid. This process is called pyrolysis and is defined as:

The chemical decomposition of a substance by heat.

The rate of pyrolysis (flammable vapour release) increases with an
increase in temperature.
Combustion Reaction
When a substance burns in air or oxygen the result is heat, light, non-
flammable gases (such as carbon dioxide CO2 and water vapour H2O) and
flammable gases (such as carbon monoxide CO). Soot will also be
produced and this is unburned fuel (carbon C) particles.

---

Flammability or Explosive Limits
Depending on the type of fuel and oxidant, a mixture which is initially at
room temperature and pressure, will burn if the concentration of fuel lies
between certain well defined limits called the
“Flammability limits” (upper & lower) or the “explosive limits” (upper &
lower).
Below the lower limit there is not enough fuel to burn often called a lean
mixture. Above the upper limit there is too much fuel to burn and the
mixture is said to be fuel rich. Within these limits there is an optimum
mixture in which there is sufficient fuel to use up all the oxygen, this is
called the stoichiometric mixture or ideal mixture as shown in Figure 1.4.

The Lower Explosive Limit (LEL) or Lower Flammability Limit (LFL)
is defined as the lowest concentration of fuel to air that will just support a
flame.

The Upper Explosive Limit (UEL) or Upper Flammability Limit (UFL)
is defined as the highest concentration of fuel to air that will just support a
flame.

The Ideal Mixture (IM) is defined as the most efficient concentration of
fuel to air that produces the highest temperatures, largest and quickest
reaction.

Flash Point

The flash point of a liquid fuel is defined as the minimum temperature of
fuel that will cause a flame to appear when an ignition source is introduced
to the fuel. The flame exists only momentarily and is then extinguished.
Essentially at flash point sufficient amounts of vapour are just being
produced to support a flame but the flame cannot be sustained. Fuels with
low flash points (such as petrol) are highly hazardous as they are
producing flammable vapours at normal temperatures.

---

Fire Point
The fire point of a liquid fuel is defined as the minimum temperature of
fuel that will cause a flame to appear and be sustained when an ignition
source is introduced to the fuel. The fire point is similar and higher than
the flashpoint. The difference being that the flame continues and is not
extinguished. Reduction of temperatures below the fire point will cause the
fire to extinguish.
Spontaneous Ignition
Certain solid fuels when heated to a certain temperature can ignite
spontaneously, that is they ignite without the presence of an ignition
source. Types of fuel where this occurs includes oily soaked rags, damp
towels in a hot press, wood shavings, animal feeds and coal heaps.
Auto- ignition Auto-ignition of gaseous fuels is defined as the temperature
at which the gas ignites spontaneously without the introduction of an
ignition source. This process also refers to the ignition of vapour from solid
fuels during pyrolysis and vapours released from liquid fuels

Extinguishing Principles
The principles of extinguishing a fire are based around the fire triangle. As
shown in Figure 1.1 we need fuel, heat and oxygen in order for combustion
to occur by removing any one of these elements the combustion process
will cease.

Depending on how this is done will influence how quickly a fire will be
extinguished. The basic extinguishing techniques are as follows:

• Starvation (limiting fuel) -  this can be achieved by either removing
potential fuel from the vicinity of fire (e.g. turning off the gas supply,
draining a burning fuel tank, cutting trenches at a bog fire or dividing
burning material into smaller fires such as pulling apart a haystack).
• Smothering (limiting oxygen) - If the oxygen supply to a burning
material can be sufficiently reduced, burning will cease. Try to
prevent fresh air from reaching the seat of the fire. This will allow
combustion to reduce the oxygen content until it extinguishes itself.
Examples are applying a foam blanket or using a fire blanket on a
burning chip pan
• Cooling (limiting temperature) - If the rate at which heat is
generated by combustion is less than the rate at which it is lost from
the burning material, burning will not continue. This can be achieved
by cooling surfaces and reducing the rate of pyrolisation, or by
applying water directly to a fire so that heat energy is converted into
energy required to convert the water into steam.

Heat Transfer
Fire fighters must understand how heat is spread (transferred) in order to
understand how fire can spread and how it can be extinguished. Heat
energy always flows from regions of high temperature to regions of lower
temperature. Heat will always flow when there is a temperature difference.
There are three methods by which heat may be transmitted, that is
conduction, convection and radiation as shown in Figure 1.6.

Conduction
Conduction may occur in solids, liquids or gases. Thermal conductivity
varies between different types of materials. Most metals conduct heat
relatively easily. During a fully developed fire an unprotected steel girder
may conduct sufficient heat to start a fire in a neighbouring compartment.
One end of the girder is heated strongly and the heat travels along the
length of the girder heating it up until the heat arrives at the far end where
it can cause combustible material in contact with it to ignite (Figure 1.7).

Convection
Convection only occurs in liquids and gases. A pan of water is heated at
the bottom, as the water warms up it expands and becomes less dense and

---

so a given volume is lighter. The heated liquid is buoyant, it rises and
colder denser fluid takes its place at the bottom. This then becomes heated
and circulation occurs. When fire occurs in buildings, convection currents
convey hot gases upward through stairwells spreading the fire to upper
parts of the building.  Another example of convection is in a domestic
chimney system

Radiation
Heat can be transferred without an intervening material (solid or liquid or
gas). Heat from the sun passes through empty space to warm the earth.
This method of transmission is known as radiation and behaves like light;
it travels in straight lines, cast shadows and will be transmitted through
some materials and not others.

The “inverse square law” states that if you double the distance from a
source of radiation the intensity of the radiation will be reduced to one
quarter. Three times the distance reduces the intensity to one ninth (see
Figure 1.10).  The “inverse square law” has serious practical implications
for fire fighters.  When conditions deteriorate or moving forward exposes
fire fighters to extreme discomfort, withdrawing even a small distance can
have a significant effect.  This is generally easier to achieve in open air
where the increased distance can be compensated for by an appropriate
increase in pressure at the branch.  However, the same principle applies
within a compartment or when moving between compartments.

Fire Development
Fire develops from ignition starting as a small fire and, given the correct
conditions, developing into a large scale fire. Fires in buildings behave
differently to fires in the open due to the effects of structural elements and the
restriction of ventilation or air. Fire fighters will usually deal with fires in a
single or a number of compartments.

---

Compartment Fires
A fire in a building will usually start in a single room. In terms of fire safety
we teach people that on discovering a fire they should evacuate the
building and close all doors behind them in order to delay the spread of fire
from the initial compartment to other compartments. Building elements
such as walls and doors are constructed to have a certain degree of fire
resistance before fire will break through. In most fires it is the spread of
smoke that kills rather than physical burning.  This fire safety advice can
result in very dangerous conditions for fire fighters entering the building
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> due to events known as flashover, backdraught and fire gas explosions. In
order to understand these risks fire fighters must understand how a fire
develops.

Fire Development in a Ventilated Compartment
A fire in a ventilated room (large ventilation opening such as an open door,
window or vent) will start small and begin to grow. As it grows the smoke
layer reaches the ceiling and begins to spread out. The smoke layer is
actually a hot fire gas layer which radiates heat downwards. The gas layer
contains soot (unburned carbon particles), and the flammable gas carbon
monoxide (CO). It also contains non-flammable gases including carbon
dioxide (CO2 and water vapour (H2O).
Heat is also radiated from compartment walls. As the fire grows taking in
oxygen from the lower area of the room near the floor, the gas layer begins
to lower towards the floor reducing visibility within the room. This gives
a smoky upper layer and a clearer lower layer. The bottom of the upper
smoky layer is called the neutral zone. Above the plane the air is said to
be in overpressure or have a slight positive pressure. Below the zone the
air is said to have an under pressure or slight negative pressure as shown
in Figure 1.12. This means that fire gases rise due to buoyancy effects.

provide a general understanding of how a compartment fire develops.
A fire’s development is mainly affected by the quantity of combustible
material and its arrangement in the fire room.
The oxygen supply is another crucial factor. If the compartment where the
fire starts is closed its intensity will gradually decrease, which means that
the temperature of the smoke gases in the compartment will drop.
In some cases, a window may crack, for instance, and the oxygen supply
provided as a result will give the fire new impetus. Concepts such as mass
loss rate of fuel and heat release rate are important

products and pyrolysis products (flammable vapours).

Fire Gas Explosion

Fire gases may be escaping from a compartment (as shown in Figure 1.17)
and may spread to other areas of the building through ducting, doorways
etc. This spread may be to either the compartment adjacent to the fire
compartment or a compartment remote from the fire. A flammable gas
mixture can build up particularly in adjacent compartments and the
introduction of an ignition source can result in a flammable gas (fire gas)
explosion. In the Blaina incident fire gases from a kitchen leaked into an
upper bedroom. The fire breaking through the bedroom floor provided the
ignition source for a deflagration.

Extinguishing Techniques
Water is an ideal medium for fire extinguishment. When applied to a fire
it attacks all sides of the fire triangle as follows:

---

•
Reduces HEAT – due to its high latent heat of evaporation it
removes heat energy from the fire as it turns to steam. When water
is heated at 100oC it expands according to the ratio 1 litre of water
to 1700 litres of steam. At increased temperatures the amount of
steam increases, for example at 500oC the expansion ratio is 1 litre
of water to 3400 litres of steam.
•
Reduces FUEL – The rapid expansion to steam dilutes the
flammable gases and also the cooling effect reduces the amount of
pyrolysis occurring thus reducing the amount of flammable vapours
being released.
•
Reducing OXYGEN – the steam produced limits the amount
of oxygen reaching the fire
(Blanketing effect)
The effectiveness of water depends very much on the method of
application. There are four methods of fire extinguishment using a branch
as follows:
•
Direct cooling
•
Painting
•
In-direct cooling
•
Gas cooling
Direct Cooling
If a jet is directed straight onto the fire large amounts of steam will be
created and visibility will be significantly reduced as the neutral plane
descends. Up to 90% of the water applied in this manner is ineffective and
not available for cooling. Large amounts of steam will make conditions for
fire fighters very uncomfortable. A jet may also have the effect of
disturbing the fire and can assist fire spread, and can also entrain further
air into the fire. Therefore the application of water is a balance between
sufficient to cool and control the fire but not enough to generate significant
amounts of steam. Direct cooling can also be used to cool combustibles
and surfaces within the compartment to reduce the rate of flammable
vapour production (pyrolysis). The spray setting and the pulse duration are
dependent on the area and cooling required.

---

Figure 1.18 – Direct Cooling
Painting
Painting is direct cooling (direct application of water onto the fire) from
close quarters once the fire has been completely knocked back to the point
of origin. Direct cooling in this situation is necessary to fully extinguish
the fire and should not generate large amounts of steam.

In-Direct Cooling
In in-direct cooling a water spray is applied to the compartment boundaries
in order to dilute the fire gases and cool the compartment boundaries. The
spray size depends on the on the compartment size and the depth of
penetration within the compartment required. For deeper penetration
narrow cone angles or jets can be used, consisting of narrow jet setting and
1 second pulses. The length of pulse may also depend on the fire size. The
theory behind the indirect attack is to aim the stream of water (30 degree
or less pattern) at the ceiling or wall and allow the droplets of water to rain
down on the fire. It has two effects:
1.
The water cools the ceiling and walls thus disrupting the thermal
balance. Some of the water will vaporise into steam thus absorbing energy.
2.
The rest will fall like rain. Some will fall on the main body of fire
to assist extinguishing the fire.

This technique will inevitably create a large amount of steam and steam
will scald fire fighters and reduce visibility. Always check your branch
setting prior to application to the fire (it may have altered since your initial
compartment entry or from when you last used it).

---

In any fire if you disrupt the thermal layer by rapidly cooling the ceiling
you will drop the thermal layer down to where you are, that means you
could be scalded. There are optimal times to use the indirect attack.  If the
fire has vented in the room of origin you may be able to push the heat and
smoke and steam out the vent (window). Because the fire products are
exiting the vent you will reduce the risk.

Compartment Procedures
Entering a compartment without taking the correct precautions can lead to
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> flashover, or back draught followed by flashover, placing the team at
serious risk. The door to a compartment, even a normal door, will provide
some protection and should remain closed. It is important to realise that
flammable fire gases may be escaping from the compartment through the
door and could be filling the corridor or space outside the compartment.
The area outside the compartment should be cooled at high level using a
pulsing spray and ventilated if safe to do so.
Temperature check
A spray is directed vertically into the ceiling. It can be used to estimate the
height of the ceiling to confirm the room type. If droplets can be heard
returning to the floor then this indicates that conditions in the ceiling gas
layer are not too hot.

Procedures inside the Compartment
Once inside the team should advance slowly but watching overhead in all
directions to assess the behaviour of the fire, particularly behind the team
to prevent the fire closing in behind them. As the team advances they
should pulse into the ceiling layer to cool the gases, reduce their
flammability, maintain the neutral zone height, and to knock back any
ceiling flame. If done correctly it is possible to knock back the flame to the
seat of the fire and then apply cooling water gently onto the seat to
extinguish (painting). The technique for ceiling pulsing is important and
can be done in two ways:
• A short pulse right and then a short pulse left
• A continuous controlled pulse rolling from right to left
The latter is more difficult to do effectively and requires practice but can
be more effective due to the fact that there is no break in the pulse. The
branch should be held in front of the face to protect the No 1, and No 2
should be sheltering behind No 1. Spray pulsing skills must be practiced
on a regular basis.
> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> Within the compartment the team can either advance or withdraw
depending on the conditions. The team can also maintain their position and
observe conditions within the compartment.

> ⚠️ **SAFETY — Action > Condition > Standard**
> The decision to advance or withdraw is taken as a result of carrying out
their personal dynamic risk assessment. This assessment is used to assess
conditions, identify where the fire is, assess the temperature at high level
(if a thermal image camera is being used), and to get a general picture of
the layout of the room and any casualties. A personal dynamic risk
assessment must be carried out on a continuous basis and every time a
compartment is entered.  The team must use gas cooling at all times to
control the fire and protect themselves. Use should also be made of any
structural fire protection available (including doors and walls). Basic
search procedures must also be used at all times.

Flame spread
The rate at which the flames spread over the material’s surface is mainly
dependent on the following:
• The material’s thermal inertia, k3c
• The surface’s direction
• The surface’s geometry
• The surrounding environment.

The flame spread rate is mainly upwards. Flame spread rate downwards is much
slower, which is due to the fact that the surface does not heat up in the same
way. In between, the rate changes according to the surface’s gradient.
In the case of vertical flame spread upwards, the height of the flames for many
materials, such as wood fibre and chipboard, is roughly twice as large over the
same period of time.
This means that if it takes 30 seconds for a 25 cm flame to grow to 50 cm, then
a 1 m high flame will grow to 2 m in about the same time, if the wall material is
the same. (This value must be regarded as only an approximation.)
The same situation applies to flame spread along the underside of a horizontal
surface as in the case of vertical flame spread upwards. In contrast to this, flame
spread on the upper part of a horizontal surface or downwards on a vertical

---

surface can be described as “creeping”, as it is slower than flame spread
upwards.

As a guide line fire/flame spread and room geometry occurs in the ratio 1:2:4
i.e.
Fire against wall will spread twice as quickly as a fire in the centre of the room
and four times as fast if it is located in the corner of the room.

Flammability in solid materials is estimated using the time it takes for ignition
to occur. The combination of properties represented by k3c refers to the
material’s thermal inertia and determines how quickly the material’s surface
heats up. The surface of a material with a low thermal inertia heats up quickly,
while the surface of one with a high k3c heats up slowly.

The lower the k3c value a material has, the shorter the ignition time. This means
that a porous wood fibre board ignites more quickly than chipboard.
A combustion process can actually be divided into a flaming fire and a
smouldering fire.
The flame spread rate is fairly slow on a surface made of a material with a high
thermal inertia (which has, more often than not, a high density). This means that
the flame spread across the surface of a heavy material is usually slower than
that across a light material.
Some of the key concepts which you need to be familiar with include unburnt
gases, premixed flames and diffusion flames. Many of the chemical processes
we will be discussing in this chapter obviously occur at other times during a
fire’s development too.
When the compartment fire grows (as the heat release rate increases), a series of
complex processes takes place in the smoke gas layer. In some cases, the smoke
gases can ignite. For this to happen, we need there to be both sufficient fuel and
oxygen present, as well as some type of ignition source.

Pyrolysis

When a solid material heats up it starts to emit gases. This process is known as
pyrolysis. Pyrolysis usually starts at temperatures in the range from 100 to 250
°C. It is pyrolysis gases which start burning when they are mixed with oxygen.
The pyrolysis process involves a chemical decomposition or chemical
conversion from complex to simpler constituents.

---

Some of the gases which accumulate at the surface of the fuel will not combust
in the flame. These unburnt gases will be accompanied by the plume and will be
contained in the smoke gas layer.
Unburnt smoke gases are always formed if combustion takes place where the
oxygen supply is insufficient. But even when the oxygen supply is sufficient for
all the fuel to combust, there are always some unburnt smoke gases formed.
The combustible products found in smoke gases originate from:
1. Pyrolysis from materials which are not in contact with the actual seat
of the fire. As the temperature is very often high up at ceiling level, combustible
ceiling material is usually pyrolysed.
2. Incomplete combustion from the actual seat of the fire. The more
incomplete the combustion is, the more combustible products there are in the
smoke gases. The poorer the access to air, the more incomplete the combustion
process is. This then increases the likelihood of the smoke gas layer igniting.

You should note that some of the potential energy available in the smoke gas
layer is very difficult to extract, even when the smoke gas layer ignites. In the
case of soot particles, for instance, the temperature needs to reach u 1000 °C for
the potential energy to be converted to heat. That explains why we often see
black smoke gases streaming out of the fire room, even if the temperature in the
room is high. Soot particles can be recognised from their black colour,

A fire in an apartment can produce a heat release rate of 2–5 MW, compared
with, for instance, a match, which can generate 80 W, or a cigarette, just 5 W.
Some release rates for other items include 1–2 MW for a sofa, and a waste
paper basket can generate a release rate of between 0.05 and 0.3 MW,
depending on its contents.

Ventilation has a major impact on combustion efficiency.

The less ventilation there is, the lower the combustion efficiency and the more
gases accumulate in the smoke gas layer. This means that in an enclosed
compartment there is a major risk of there being a lot of unburnt gases. This is
also due, of course, to the fact that other surfaces have pyrolysed. During the
last few decades, the shift from using wood based materials to synthetic
polymers has brought about a new situation in the area of fire protection.
